EXAM STRUCTURE (Series 18-03, 150 questions, ONE combined Life + Health exam): Insurance Regulation, General Insurance, Life Insurance Basics, Life Policies, Life Provisions/Riders, Annuities, Federal Tax (Life), Qualified Plans, then Health Basics, Individual Health Provisions, Disability Income, Medical Plans, Group Health, Dental, Seniors/Special Needs, and Federal Tax (Health). Connecticut statutes are in Title 38a.

SECTION 1: INSURANCE REGULATION (Connecticut)
Continuing education: 24 hours per 2-year license term, including at least 3 hours of CT insurance law/regulation or ethics, and at least 6 hours per line of authority. Producer licensing, appointments, and unfair trade practices sit in Title 38a (38a-702 series; unfair practices 38a-815 et seq), the standard NAIC framework. Rebating (giving or receiving) is prohibited. Guaranty Association gag rule: a producer may not use the Connecticut Life & Health Insurance Guaranty Association's existence as a sales inducement [[C.G.S. 38a-858]].

SECTION 2: GENERAL INSURANCE
Risk = uncertainty of loss; pure risk (loss or no loss) is insurable, speculative (gambling) is not. Peril = cause of loss; hazard = condition increasing loss (physical/moral/morale). STARR methods: Sharing, Transfer, Avoidance, Retention, Reduction. Law of large numbers; adverse selection; reinsurance. Stock insurers = nonparticipating; mutual = participating (policy dividends, not taxable). Domestic/foreign/alien = this state/another state/another country. Admitted = holds a certificate of authority. Agent authority: express, implied, apparent. Contract elements: offer/acceptance, consideration, competent parties, legal purpose. Characteristics: adhesion (ambiguities against the insurer), aleatory, unilateral, conditional. Life insurance is a valued contract; health is a reimbursement (indemnity) contract. Representations (believed true, void only if material) vs warranties (guaranteed true). Waiver vs estoppel. Utmost good faith.

SECTION 3: LIFE INSURANCE BASICS
Insurable interest must exist at the time of APPLICATION (inception), not at death. Human life value = replace future earnings; needs approach = total needs minus resources, best for how much to apply for. Business uses (buy-sell): partnership cross-purchase (each partner insures the others) vs entity plan (the partnership owns); close corporation cross-purchase vs stock redemption (corporation owns). Key person: the business owns, pays, and is beneficiary; premiums NOT deductible, death proceeds NOT taxable. Premiums: factors are mortality, interest, expense; more frequent mode = higher total cost. Underwriting sources: application (primary), APS, MIB (cannot decline on MIB alone), inspection/consumer report (FCRA notice + right to a copy). Risk classes: preferred, standard, substandard (rated), declined.

SECTION 4: LIFE INSURANCE POLICIES
Term = cheapest, no cash value, pays only during the term. Level/decreasing (mortgage)/increasing/annual renewable. Renewable and convertible both reprice at attained age. Whole life = permanent, matures at 100, level premium, cash value; types differ only in how paid (straight, limited-pay, single-premium, modified, graded). Universal life = flexible premium and adjustable death benefit, minimum guaranteed interest. Variable life/VUL are securities (separate account, owner bears risk), require FINRA registration plus a life license. Special policies: family plan, family income (decreasing term from purchase) vs family maintenance (level term from death), joint life (first-to-die) vs survivorship (second-to-die), juvenile (payor provision), credit life (decreasing term, capped at the loan). MEC: fails the 7-pay test; taxed LIFO with a 10% penalty before 59 1/2.

SECTION 5: LIFE POLICY PROVISIONS, OPTIONS AND RIDERS (Connecticut values)
Free look: 10 days to return an individual life policy; on return it is void ab initio [[C.G.S. 38a-436]]. Standard Nonforfeiture Law options: cash surrender, extended term (the automatic option), reduced paid-up (38a-438/439). Standard provisions: entire contract, incontestability (typically 2 years), grace period, reinstatement, misstatement of age (adjusts the benefit, never voids). Assignment: absolute vs collateral (creditor reimbursed, beneficiary keeps the excess). Settlement options: cash, interest only, fixed-period, fixed-amount, life income. Dividend options (participating only; dividends are a non-taxable return of premium): cash, reduced premium, accumulate at interest, paid-up additions, one-year term. Riders: waiver of premium (total/permanent disability, primary insured), accidental death (double indemnity), AD&D (principal sum for two losses, capital sum for one), guaranteed insurability, cost of living, payor, return of premium, accelerated benefits (terminal illness, reduces death benefit). Guaranty Association: CT covers life death benefit $500,000, cash surrender $500,000, and the aggregate cap is $500,000 per insured life [[C.G.S. 38a-858]].

SECTION 6: ANNUITIES
An annuity protects against outliving your money. Owner, annuitant (measuring life), beneficiary. Immediate (single premium, pays within about a month) vs deferred (single or flexible, pays after at least a year). Fixed (guaranteed rate, insurer bears risk, general account) vs variable (separate account, owner bears risk, requires FINRA plus a license). Equity-indexed = a fixed annuity with index-linked excess interest and a floor. Payout options: straight life (most income, nothing to a beneficiary), life with period certain, cash/installment refund, joint and survivor. Exclusion ratio = investment divided by expected return (the tax-free share). Annuity guaranty limit in CT: $500,000 present value.

SECTION 7: FEDERAL TAX (Life & Annuities)
Death benefit is income-tax-free to the beneficiary. Cash value grows tax-deferred. Dividends are a non-taxable return of premium (interest earned is taxable). Policy loans are not taxable in force. MEC: fails the 7-pay test, taxed LIFO, 10% penalty before 59 1/2; death benefit stays tax-free. Non-qualified annuity: exclusion ratio splits tax-free principal from taxable earnings; pre-59 1/2 distributions face a 10% penalty and are LIFO. Traditional IRA: deductible in, taxed out. Roth: after-tax in, tax-free out (5-year hold plus 59 1/2, death, disability, or first home $10,000 max). 1035 exchange: life to life, life to annuity, annuity to annuity tax-free; annuity to life is NOT allowed.

SECTION 8: QUALIFIED PLANS
Qualified = meets IRS rules, so contributions are deductible, growth is tax-deferred, distributions are taxed as ordinary income. ERISA sets participation, vesting, funding, and fiduciary standards. Defined benefit (formula sets the benefit, employer risk) vs defined contribution (formula sets the input, employee risk). Plan types: SEP (employer funds the employee's IRA), Keogh/HR-10 (self-employed), 401(k) (pre-tax deferral), 403(b)/TSA (public schools, nonprofits, ministers), SIMPLE (small employers).

SECTION 9: HEALTH INSURANCE BASICS
Perils and hazards as above. Policy classes: individual vs group, and limited policies (accident-only, hospital indemnity paying a fixed daily amount, specified/dread disease, credit). Accidental means vs accidental results (results is broader and more favorable). Principal sum vs capital sum (AD&D). HSA requires a qualified high-deductible plan; triple tax advantage, funds roll over and are portable. Underwriting sources mirror life (application, MIB, APS, consumer reports under FCRA).

SECTION 10: INDIVIDUAL HEALTH POLICY PROVISIONS (Connecticut values) — C.G.S. 38a-483
Connecticut's required standard provisions for individual health policies:
Grace period: at least 7 days for weekly-premium policies, 10 days for monthly-premium, 31 days for all other policies. Time limit on certain defenses (incontestability): the policy is incontestable, except for nonpayment, after 2 years from issue. Reinstatement: a later premium acceptance reinstates; if an application and conditional receipt are used, the policy reinstates on approval or on the 45th day after the conditional receipt unless disapproved; a reinstated policy covers accidental injury immediately and sickness beginning more than 10 days after, and premium may be applied no more than 60 days before reinstatement. The NAIC mandatory uniform provisions also include entire contract, notice of claim, claim forms, proof of loss, time of payment of claims, and physical exam/autopsy [[C.G.S. 38a-483]].

SECTION 11: DISABILITY INCOME
Own-occupation (more favorable: pays if you cannot do YOUR occupation) vs any-occupation (pays only if you cannot do ANY suitable occupation). Elimination period (waiting period, no benefits) vs benefit period. Residual/partial disability pays a proportional benefit for lost income. Riders: additional monthly benefit, Social Insurance Supplement, cost of living, future increase option. Business DI: key person, business overhead expense (BOE, pays business expenses), disability buy-sell. Social Security disability: strict any-occupation definition, 5-month elimination period.

SECTION 12: MEDICAL PLANS
HMO (closed network, prepaid, gatekeeper PCP controls referrals, emergency care needs no prior authorization) vs PPO (open panel, in/out-of-network tiers, gatekeeper optional) vs indemnity (any provider, URC reimbursement). Service plans (Blue Cross Blue Shield) contract with providers. HIPAA: creditable coverage, guaranteed issue/renewability for groups. Utilization review: prospective, concurrent, retrospective. ACA: guaranteed issue and renewability, $0 in-network preventive care, no lifetime/annual dollar limits on essential health benefits, out-of-pocket maximums, emergency care without prior authorization. Mental health parity (federal MHPAEA): parity if mental health is covered.

SECTION 13: GROUP HEALTH INSURANCE
Group coverage uses a master contract; the group is underwritten as a whole, usually with no evidence of insurability. Coordination of benefits prevents over-payment (the birthday rule decides primary for dependent children). COBRA (federal) lets qualified employees continue group coverage after a qualifying event. ERISA governs private employer plans. Conversion lets a departing member convert to an individual policy.

SECTION 14: DENTAL INSURANCE
Typical coinsurance tiers: 100% preventive / 80% basic / 50% major. Predetermination (pre-treatment estimate) for large work. Watch adverse selection. Scheduled vs comprehensive (integrated deductible) plans.

SECTION 15: SENIORS AND SPECIAL NEEDS
Medicare: Part A (hospital, mostly premium-free), Part B (medical, monthly premium), Part C (Medicare Advantage), Part D (drugs). Medicare Supplement (Medigap) fills gaps and uses standardized lettered plans with open enrollment tied to turning 65 and enrolling in Part B. Long-term care insurance covers custodial care, triggered by inability to perform activities of daily living (ADLs) or cognitive impairment. Medicaid is a needs-based state/federal program.

SECTION 16: FEDERAL TAX (Health)
Personally-owned disability income: premiums NOT deductible, benefits TAX-FREE. Employer-paid group disability: premiums deductible to the employer, benefits TAXABLE to the employee. Employer-paid group medical: deductible to employer, not income to employees, benefits tax-free (the most tax-favored arrangement). Self-employed health insurance: above-the-line deduction. Key person and disability buy-sell: premiums not deductible, benefits not taxable. Business overhead expense (BOE): premiums ARE deductible, benefits ARE taxable (the inversion).
