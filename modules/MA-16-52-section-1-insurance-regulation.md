# Massachusetts — Accident & Health Producer Exam (Series 16-52)
## Module 1.0: Insurance Regulation
**Exam weight: 9% (≈9 of 100 questions) | Source law: M.G.L. c. 175 §§ 162G–162X, c. 176D | Verified against Prometric MA Licensing Information Handbook, outline live date Aug 25, 2018 (current edition)**

> **How to study this module:** Massachusetts adopted the NAIC Producer Licensing Model Act as M.G.L. c. 175 §§ 162G–162X, so the licensing mechanics follow the national pattern with MA citations. The regulation questions cluster around three themes: getting and keeping a license (1.1), what the Commissioner and the unfair practices law prohibit (1.2), and two federal laws (1.3). Memorize the bolded numbers and the 14 grounds for discipline.

## Part 1: Licensing (Outline 1.1)

### Who needs a license
No person may **sell, solicit, or negotiate** insurance in Massachusetts without a producer license for the relevant line of authority (175:162I). Massachusetts requires no pre-licensing coursework (175:162L) — candidates must be at least **18**, pass the Prometric exam for their line, and apply through NIPR. Exams are required for the major lines (Life, A&H, Property, Casualty, Personal Lines); the Variable line requires holding the Life line plus FINRA registration rather than a separate state exam, and Travel requires no exam.

### Lines of authority — M.G.L. c. 175 § 162M(a)
A producer may qualify in one or more of: **Life; Accident and Health or Sickness; Property; Casualty; Variable Life and Variable Annuity; Personal Lines; Credit (limited line); Travel (limited line)**. The license is valid only for the lines named on it.
- *Exam focus:* the A&H line covers sickness, bodily injury, and accidental death, and **may include disability income benefits** — but note disability income can also ride under the Life line.

### Keeping the license — M.G.L. c. 175 § 162M(b)–(f)
- The license **remains in effect indefinitely** as long as the renewal fee is paid and CE requirements are met by the due date. (Initial license runs 3 years or less, pegged to the licensee's birthday.)
- **Lapsed license:** may be reinstated within **12 months** of the renewal due date **without retaking the exam**, but with a penalty of **double the unpaid renewal fee**.
- **Military/extenuating circumstances:** a producer may request a waiver of renewal procedures, exams, fines, or sanctions.
- **Address change:** must be reported to the Commissioner within **30 days**.
- **Assumed (DBA) names:** must be reported to the Commissioner before use (175:162P).
- **Reporting actions (175:162V):** a producer must report to the Commissioner any **administrative action** taken against them in another jurisdiction (or by another MA agency) within **30 days of final disposition**, including a copy of the order or consent documents — and must report any **criminal prosecution** in any jurisdiction within **30 days of the initial pretrial hearing date**, including the complaint and resulting orders.
- **Temporary license (175:162Q):** the Commissioner may issue a temporary producer license for up to **180 days, without an examination**, to keep an insurance business running — for the surviving spouse or personal representative of a producer who dies or becomes disabled, for a designee of a business entity's key individual in the same circumstances, for the designee of a producer entering active military service, or in other public-interest cases. The Commissioner may require a **sponsor** (a licensed producer or insurer) who assumes responsibility for the temporary licensee's acts, and the temporary license ends when the business is sold.

### Continuing education
- **60 hours** before the first license renewal, including **3 hours of MA-approved Ethics**.
- **45 hours** each subsequent 36-month period, also including **3 hours of Ethics**.
- One-time 3-hour Flood Insurance CE applies only to P&C/Personal Lines producers selling NFIP — not A&H.

### Discipline — M.G.L. c. 175 § 162R
The Commissioner may **place on probation, suspend, revoke, or refuse to issue or renew** a license, and/or levy a civil penalty under c. 176D § 7, for any of **14 grounds**:

1. Incorrect, misleading, incomplete, or materially untrue information on the license application
2. Violating any insurance law, regulation, subpoena, or order (of MA or any state's commissioner)
3. Obtaining or attempting to obtain a license through misrepresentation or fraud
4. Improperly withholding, misappropriating, or converting money or property received in the business
5. Intentionally misrepresenting the terms of an actual or proposed contract or application
6. Felony conviction
7. Admitting or being found to have committed an unfair trade practice or insurance fraud
8. Fraudulent, coercive, or dishonest practices, or demonstrating incompetence, untrustworthiness, or financial irresponsibility — in Massachusetts **or anywhere else**
9. Having a producer license denied, suspended, or revoked in another jurisdiction
10. Forging a name on an application or insurance document
11. Cheating on a licensing exam (improper use of notes or reference material)
12. Knowingly accepting insurance business from an unlicensed individual
13. Failing to comply with a child support order
14. Failing to pay state income tax or comply with an order to pay it

Key procedural rights and rules:
- If the Commissioner denies or nonrenews, the applicant may make **written demand within 30 days** for a hearing (held under Chapter 30A).
- A **business entity's** license can be disciplined if a partner, officer, or manager **knew or should have known** of an individual licensee's violation and neither reported it nor took corrective action.
- Surrendering or letting a license lapse does **not** escape enforcement — the Commissioner retains authority over anyone under investigation.
- **Fines (verified):** unfair or deceptive acts under c. 176D carry a fine of up to **$1,000 for each and every act or practice** plus a cease-and-desist order, with license suspension — or revocation for **repeated** violations (176D:7). Violating a final cease-and-desist order costs up to **$10,000 per violation** (176D:10). Violations of insurance laws not separately listed (such as failing to report under 162V) draw up to **$500 per violation** under 175:194, while violations of insurance laws, regulations, or orders under 162R(a)(2) support fines up to **$1,000 each**.

## Part 2: State Regulation (Outline 1.2)

### The Commissioner
The Commissioner of Insurance heads the Division of Insurance and **enforces** the insurance laws (175:3A; 176D:5) — issuing regulations, approving forms, examining companies, licensing producers, and holding hearings. The Commissioner is appointed, not elected, and enforces rather than writes the statutes.

### Company regulation
- **Certificate of authority** (175:4, 32, 151): a company must hold one to transact insurance in MA. Admitted/authorized = holds a certificate; nonadmitted = doesn't.
- **Policy forms** (175:2B, 192): forms, riders, and endorsements must be filed with and approved by the Commissioner before use.
- **Examination of books and records** (175:4): the Commissioner may examine insurer records.
- **Producer appointments — M.G.L. c. 175 § 162S:** a producer cannot act as an insurer's *agent* without being appointed. The insurer must file the notice of appointment within **15 days** of the agency contract being executed **or** the first application being submitted, whichever comes first. The Commissioner verifies eligibility within **30 days** and notifies the insurer within **5 days** if the producer is ineligible. The insurer pays the appointment fee.
- **Termination of appointment — M.G.L. c. 175 § 162T:** an insurer that terminates a producer's appointment, employment, or contract must notify the Commissioner within **30 days of the termination's effective date** — whether the termination is for one of the 162R causes or for any other reason (both carry the same 30-day clock; for-cause terminations require the prescribed format and supporting records on request). Within **15 days** after notifying the Commissioner, the insurer must mail a copy of that notification to the producer at the last known address — by **certified mail, return receipt requested**, when the termination was for a 162R cause. If the insurer later discovers reportable information, it must promptly supplement its notice.

### Producer conduct crimes
- **Impersonation** (175:175): impersonating another in an insurance transaction is a criminal offense.
- **Larceny** (175:176): premiums a producer collects are held in a **fiduciary capacity** — converting them is larceny.
- **Unlicensed compensation** (175:177): paying commission to, or accepting business from, unlicensed persons is prohibited.

### Unfair and deceptive practices — M.G.L. c. 176D § 3
The heart of this section. Prohibited acts include:
- **Misrepresentation and false advertising** — misstating policy terms, benefits, or dividends, or making untrue/deceptive statements about the business of insurance
- **Defamation** — false or malicious statements injuring another insurer
- **Boycott, coercion, intimidation** — unreasonable restraint of the insurance business
- **False financial statements**
- **Failure to maintain a complaint record** — insurers must keep a record of all complaints received
- **Unfair discrimination** — between individuals of the same class and equal expectation of life (or same class/hazard in A&H) in rates, benefits, or terms
- **Rebating** (175:182–184; 176D:3(8)) — offering anything of value not specified in the policy as an inducement to buy (premium rebates, gifts, securities, special favors). Both **giving and receiving** a rebate are prohibited.
- **Unfair claims settlement practices** (176D:3(9)) — the most-tested list. Committed when done with such frequency as to indicate a business practice: misrepresenting policy facts; failing to acknowledge and act promptly on communications; failing to adopt reasonable standards for prompt claim investigation; refusing to pay without a reasonable investigation; failing to affirm or deny coverage within a reasonable time after proof of loss; failing to make a prompt, fair, and equitable settlement once liability is reasonably clear; forcing insureds to sue by offering substantially less than the amounts ultimately recovered; and failing to promptly explain the basis for a denial.

### Privacy — M.G.L. c. 175I
The Insurance Information and Privacy Protection Act governs how insurers and producers collect, use, and disclose personal information: applicants must receive a **notice of information practices**, may access and request correction of recorded personal information, and must receive specific reasons for adverse underwriting decisions. Investigative consumer reports require disclosure, and the applicant may request an interview as part of the report.

## Part 3: Federal Regulation (Outline 1.3)

### Fair Credit Reporting Act (15 USC 1681)
Governs consumer reports used in underwriting. Applicants must be told a report may be requested; if an **investigative consumer report** (interviews with neighbors, associates) is ordered, the applicant must be notified and may request disclosure of its nature and scope. If insurance is declined or rated up based on a report (**adverse action**), the insurer must tell the applicant and identify the reporting agency. Consumers may dispute and correct inaccurate information.

### Federal fraud and false statements — 18 USC 1033 & 1034
- It is a **federal crime** for anyone engaged in the business of insurance to make false statements or reports, embezzle, or willfully overvalue assets in financial documents.
- A person convicted of a **felony involving dishonesty or breach of trust** is **prohibited** from working in the business of insurance unless they obtain **written consent** from an insurance regulator — the **"1033 waiver."** In MA, the home-state consent question appears right on the NAIC license application background questions.
- 18 USC 1034 provides civil penalties and injunctive authority for 1033 violations.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A producer's license lapsed 8 months ago. What must she do to get it back?
A) Retake the licensing exam  B) Pay double the unpaid renewal fee — no exam required  C) Complete 60 new CE hours first  D) Wait 12 months and reapply
> **B.** Within 12 months of the renewal due date, a lapsed license is reinstated without re-examination, but the penalty is double the unpaid renewal fee. (175:162M(c))

**2.** An insurer signs an agency contract with a newly licensed producer on March 1 and receives her first application March 3. By when must the appointment notice be filed?
A) March 8  B) March 16  C) March 31  D) Before the first policy is issued
> **B.** The notice of appointment is due within 15 days of the contract execution or first application submission. (175:162S(b))

**3.** Which of the following is NOT among the grounds for license suspension or revocation under 175:162R?
A) Felony conviction  B) Failure to pay state income tax  C) Cheating on the licensing exam  D) Failing to meet a monthly sales quota
> **D.** Production levels are between the producer and insurer; the 14 statutory grounds cover dishonesty, legal violations, fiduciary breaches, and compliance failures.

**4.** A producer offers a client two free Bruins tickets if she buys a health policy. This is:
A) Permissible relationship marketing  B) Twisting  C) Illegal rebating  D) Coercion
> **C.** Offering anything of value not specified in the policy as an inducement to purchase is rebating, prohibited under 175:182–184 and 176D:3(8) — for both the giver and the receiver.

**5.** An insurer routinely waits months to acknowledge claim letters and denies claims without investigating. Under M.G.L. c. 176D § 3(9), this is:
A) Defamation  B) An unfair claims settlement practice  C) False advertising  D) Permissible cost containment
> **B.** Failing to act promptly on communications and refusing to pay claims without reasonable investigation are unfair claims settlement practices when frequent enough to indicate a business practice.

**6.** A candidate was convicted five years ago of felony embezzlement. Under federal law, before working as an insurance producer he must:
A) Wait 10 years from conviction  B) Obtain written consent from an insurance regulator (a 1033 waiver)  C) Disclose it only if asked  D) Nothing — state licensing law controls
> **B.** 18 USC 1033 bars anyone convicted of a felony involving dishonesty or breach of trust from the business of insurance without a regulator's written consent.
