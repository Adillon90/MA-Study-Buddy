## Part 1: Risk Concepts (Outline 2.1)

### The key terms — keep them apart
- **Risk** — uncertainty about loss. **Pure risk** (loss or no loss only — insurable) vs. **speculative risk** (chance of gain, like gambling — NOT insurable).
- **Exposure** — a unit of measure of loss potential.
- **Peril** — the **cause** of loss (sickness, accident, fire).
- **Hazard** — a condition that **increases the chance** of loss, in three flavors:
  - **Physical** — tangible condition (icy stairs, a heart condition)
  - **Moral** — dishonesty (a history of fraud)
  - **Morale** — carelessness/indifference *because* insurance exists ("it's covered, why lock it")
- **Loss** — the unintentional reduction in value.

### Methods of handling risk — STARR
**S**haring, **T**ransfer (insurance is risk transfer), **A**voidance, **R**etention (self-insuring, deductibles), **R**eduction (sprinklers, wellness programs).

### Elements of an insurable risk
Loss must be: **due to chance** (outside the insured's control), **definite and measurable**, **statistically predictable**, **not catastrophic** to the insurer, part of a **large homogeneous group**, and the premium must be **economically feasible**.

### The actuarial backbone
- **Law of large numbers:** the larger the pool of similar exposures, the more accurately losses can be predicted — the mathematical foundation of insurance.
- **Adverse selection:** the tendency of higher-risk people to seek and keep insurance more than low-risk people. Underwriting exists to fight it.
- **Reinsurance:** insurers insuring insurers — the ceding company transfers part of a risk to a reinsurer, spreading catastrophic exposure.

## Part 2: Insurers (Outline 2.2)

- **Stock company** — owned by stockholders; traditionally issues **nonparticipating** policies (no dividends to policyholders).
- **Mutual company** — owned by **policyholders**; issues **participating** policies that may pay **policy dividends** (treated as a return of excess premium — not taxable income).
- **Fraternal benefit societies** — nonprofit member organizations (lodge system) selling to members.
- **Risk retention / risk purchasing groups and self-insurance groups** — alternatives where members pool or retain their own risk.
- **Private vs. government insurers** — commercial market vs. Medicare, Medicaid, Social Security.
- **Admitted (authorized)** — holds a MA certificate of authority — vs. **nonadmitted**.
- **Domestic / foreign / alien** — incorporated in this state / another **state** / another **country**. (A Connecticut insurer is *foreign* in MA; a Canadian insurer is *alien*.)
- **Financial ratings** — independent services (A.M. Best, S&P, Moody's, Fitch) grade insurer solvency.
- **Marketing systems** — independent agency (represents several insurers, owns expirations), exclusive/captive agency (one insurer), direct writer (employee producers), direct response (no producer — mail/phone/internet).

## Part 3: Producers and Agency Law (Outline 2.3)

- The **insurer is the principal**; the appointed producer is its agent. The producer's acts within authority are legally **the insurer's acts**, and knowledge gained by the agent is imputed to the insurer.
- **Three kinds of authority:**
  - **Express** — explicitly granted in the written agency contract
  - **Implied** — not written, but reasonably necessary to carry out express authority (collecting premiums, using letterhead)
  - **Apparent** — authority a reasonable consumer **believes** the agent has because the insurer allowed the appearance (unreturned rate books, signage). The insurer can be bound by it.
- The producer simultaneously owes the applicant honesty, suitability, and prompt handling — the "responsibilities to the applicant/insured" thread that runs through E&O law.

## Part 4: Contracts (Outline 2.4)

### Four elements of any legal contract
1. **Offer and acceptance** (the application is the offer; the insurer accepts by issuing)
2. **Consideration** — the applicant's premium + statements; the insurer's promise to pay
3. **Competent parties** — legal age, sound mind, not under duress
4. **Legal purpose** — insurable interest and consent keep insurance from being a wager

### Distinct characteristics of insurance contracts
- **Adhesion** — drafted entirely by the insurer, take-it-or-leave-it → **ambiguities are construed against the insurer** (and the related doctrine of **reasonable expectations** protects what a buyer could fairly believe was covered)
- **Aleatory** — unequal exchange of values; performance depends on chance (a small premium may yield a huge claim, or nothing)
- **Personal** — covers the person, not transferable without consent
- **Conditional** — the insurer pays only if conditions (premium paid, proof of loss) are met
- **Unilateral** — only the insurer makes a legally enforceable promise

### Truth-telling doctrines
- **Indemnity** — restore, don't enrich; **utmost good faith** — both parties rely on honesty
- **Representations** — statements believed true (the legal status of application answers); a **misrepresentation** voids coverage only if **material**
- **Warranties** — statements guaranteed absolutely true (rare in modern A&H)
- **Concealment** — withholding a known material fact; **fraud** — intentional deception for gain
- **Waiver** — the intentional surrender of a known right; **estoppel** — the law preventing a party from reasserting a right it waived (an insurer that knowingly accepts premiums on a defective application may be estopped from later denying coverage)

## Practice Questions

**1.** A policyholder leaves his door unlocked, figuring "insurance will cover it." This is an example of:
A) Moral hazard  B) Morale hazard  C) Physical hazard  D) Peril
> **B.** Indifference or carelessness arising from being insured is morale hazard; moral hazard is dishonesty; the burglary itself would be the peril. (Outline 2.1)

**2.** An insurer incorporated in Ontario, Canada and selling in Massachusetts is classified in MA as a(n):
A) Domestic insurer  B) Foreign insurer  C) Alien insurer  D) Nonadmitted insurer by definition
> **C.** Incorporated outside the U.S. = alien. Another U.S. state = foreign. (It may still be admitted if it holds a MA certificate of authority.) (Outline 2.2)

**3.** A producer's contract says nothing about collecting initial premiums, but doing so is customary and necessary to transact the business. The producer's authority to collect is:
A) Express  B) Implied  C) Apparent  D) Nonexistent
> **B.** Implied authority covers acts reasonably necessary to carry out express authority even though unwritten. (Outline 2.3)

**4.** The insurance contract characteristic that explains why courts construe ambiguous policy language against the insurer is that the contract is:
A) Aleatory  B) Unilateral  C) A contract of adhesion  D) Conditional
> **C.** The insurer drafted every word and the buyer couldn't negotiate — so ambiguity is resolved in the insured's favor. (Outline 2.4)

**5.** An insured pays $800 in premium and collects $90,000 for a covered loss. The unequal exchange illustrates that insurance contracts are:
A) Conditional  B) Aleatory  C) Personal  D) Contracts of indemnity
> **B.** Aleatory contracts involve exchanges of unequal value contingent on an uncertain event. (Outline 2.4)

**6.** For two years an insurer knowingly accepted premiums despite a defect that would have allowed it to void the policy. At claim time it tries to deny on that defect. The doctrine most likely to defeat the denial is:
A) Subrogation  B) Estoppel  C) Adverse selection  D) Indemnity
> **B.** Having waived the right by knowingly accepting premiums, the insurer is estopped from reasserting it. (Outline 2.4)
