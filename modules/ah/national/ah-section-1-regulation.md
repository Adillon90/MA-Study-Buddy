## Part 1: Licensing (Outline 1.1)

Most states adopted the **NAIC Producer Licensing Model Act**, so the licensing mechanics follow a national pattern; only the values differ by state.

### Who needs a license
No person may **sell, solicit, or negotiate** insurance without a producer license for the relevant **line of authority**. "Sell, solicit, or negotiate" is the model-act trigger phrase. Licensure generally requires meeting a **minimum age**, passing the state exam for the line, and applying (most states route applications through **NIPR**). Pre-licensing education requirements vary by state (some require classroom hours, some none — see overlay).

### Lines of authority (NAIC model § 2)
A producer may qualify in one or more of: **Life; Accident and Health or Sickness; Property; Casualty; Variable Life and Variable Annuity; Personal Lines; Credit (limited line); Travel (limited line)**. The license is valid only for the lines named on it.
- *Exam focus:* the A&H line covers sickness, bodily injury, and accidental death, and **may include disability income benefits** — though disability income can also ride under the Life line.
- The **Variable** line typically requires holding the Life line plus **FINRA registration** rather than a separate state exam; **Travel** is a limited line that usually requires no exam.

### Keeping the license
- A license generally **remains in effect** as long as the renewal fee is paid and **continuing education (CE)** is completed by the due date. Initial license terms and renewal cycles are state-set (commonly pegged to the licensee's birthday).
- **Lapsed license:** most states allow reinstatement within a set window **without retaking the exam**, usually with a penalty (e.g., a multiple of the unpaid fee). The window and penalty are state-specific.
- **Military/extenuating circumstances:** a producer may request a waiver of renewal procedures, exams, fines, or sanctions.
- **Address change:** must be reported to the regulator within a state-set number of days.
- **Assumed (DBA) names:** must be reported before use.
- **Reporting actions (NAIC model § 13):** a producer must report **administrative actions** taken against them in another jurisdiction and any **criminal prosecution**, within a state-set deadline, with copies of the orders/charging documents.
- **Temporary license (NAIC model § 10):** the regulator may issue a temporary producer license **without an examination** for a limited period to keep an insurance business running — for the surviving spouse/personal representative of a producer who dies or becomes disabled, the designee of a business entity's key person in the same circumstances, the designee of a producer entering active military service, or other public-interest cases. The regulator may require a **sponsor** who assumes responsibility for the temporary licensee's acts; the license ends when the business is sold.

### Continuing education
CE is universal, but the **hours, ethics-hours requirement, and cycle length are state-set** (see overlay). The common model: a larger first-renewal requirement, then a recurring per-cycle requirement, each including a few hours of approved **Ethics**. (A one-time Flood Insurance CE applies only to P&C/Personal Lines producers selling NFIP — not A&H.)

### Discipline (NAIC model § 12)
The regulator may **place on probation, suspend, revoke, or refuse to issue or renew** a license, and/or levy a civil penalty, for grounds the NAIC model enumerates and most states adopted nearly verbatim:

1. Incorrect, misleading, incomplete, or materially untrue information on the license application
2. Violating any insurance law, regulation, subpoena, or order (of this or any state's regulator)
3. Obtaining or attempting to obtain a license through misrepresentation or fraud
4. Improperly withholding, misappropriating, or converting money or property received in the business
5. Intentionally misrepresenting the terms of an actual or proposed contract or application
6. Felony conviction
7. Admitting or being found to have committed an unfair trade practice or insurance fraud
8. Fraudulent, coercive, or dishonest practices, or demonstrating incompetence, untrustworthiness, or financial irresponsibility — in-state **or anywhere else**
9. Having a producer license denied, suspended, or revoked in another jurisdiction
10. Forging a name on an application or insurance document
11. Cheating on a licensing exam (improper use of notes or reference material)
12. Knowingly accepting insurance business from an unlicensed individual
13. Failing to comply with a child support order
14. Failing to pay state income tax or comply with an order to pay it

(States vary slightly in how they number and word these; the exact enumeration and the fine amounts are in the overlay.)

Key procedural rights and rules (model-uniform):
- A denied/nonrenewed applicant may make **written demand** for a hearing within a state-set period (often under the state administrative procedure act).
- A **business entity's** license can be disciplined if a partner, officer, or manager **knew or should have known** of an individual licensee's violation and neither reported it nor took corrective action.
- Surrendering or letting a license lapse does **not** escape enforcement — the regulator retains authority over anyone under investigation.

## Part 2: State Regulation (Outline 1.2)

### The regulator
Each state's insurance department is headed by a **Commissioner** (or Superintendent/Director) who **enforces** the insurance laws — issuing regulations, approving forms, examining companies, licensing producers, and holding hearings. The Commissioner is typically appointed (a few states elect the position) and **enforces** rather than writes the statutes.

### Company regulation (universal mechanics)
- **Certificate of authority:** a company must hold one to transact insurance in the state. Admitted/authorized = holds a certificate; nonadmitted = doesn't.
- **Policy forms:** forms, riders, and endorsements must be **filed with and approved** by the regulator before use.
- **Examination of books and records:** the regulator may examine insurer records.
- **Producer appointments (NAIC model):** a producer cannot act as an insurer's *agent* without being **appointed**. The insurer files a notice of appointment within a state-set window after the agency contract is executed or the first application is submitted; the regulator verifies eligibility and the insurer pays the appointment fee. (Day-counts in overlay.)
- **Termination of appointment (NAIC model):** an insurer terminating a producer's appointment, employment, or contract must notify the regulator within a state-set window of the effective date — whether for cause (one of the disciplinary grounds) or any other reason — and must mail a copy of that notice to the producer (certified mail when for cause). If it later discovers reportable information, it must supplement. (Day-counts in overlay.)

### Producer conduct crimes (universal)
- **Impersonation:** impersonating another in an insurance transaction is a criminal offense.
- **Fiduciary duty / larceny:** premiums a producer collects are held in a **fiduciary capacity** — converting them is theft.
- **Unlicensed compensation:** paying commission to, or accepting business from, unlicensed persons is prohibited.

### Unfair and deceptive practices (NAIC Unfair Trade Practices Act)
The heart of this section, adopted nearly uniformly. Prohibited acts include:
- **Misrepresentation and false advertising** — misstating policy terms, benefits, or dividends, or making untrue/deceptive statements about the business of insurance.
- **Defamation** — false or malicious statements injuring another insurer.
- **Boycott, coercion, intimidation** — unreasonable restraint of the insurance business.
- **False financial statements.**
- **Failure to maintain a complaint record** — insurers must keep a record of all complaints received.
- **Unfair discrimination** — between individuals of the same class and equal expectation of life (or same class/hazard in A&H) in rates, benefits, or terms.
- **Rebating** — offering anything of value not specified in the policy as an inducement to buy (premium rebates, gifts, securities, special favors). Both **giving and receiving** a rebate are prohibited. (A few states have repealed or narrowed rebate bans — confirm in overlay.)
- **Unfair claims settlement practices** (NAIC Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act) — the most-tested list. Committed when done with such frequency as to indicate a **business practice**: misrepresenting policy facts; failing to acknowledge and act promptly on communications; failing to adopt reasonable standards for prompt claim investigation; refusing to pay without a reasonable investigation; failing to affirm or deny coverage within a reasonable time after proof of loss; failing to make a prompt, fair, and equitable settlement once liability is reasonably clear; forcing insureds to sue by offering substantially less than the amounts ultimately recovered; and failing to promptly explain the basis for a denial.

### Privacy (NAIC Insurance Information and Privacy Protection Model Act)
Most states have a privacy statute governing how insurers and producers collect, use, and disclose personal information: applicants must receive a **notice of information practices**, may access and request correction of recorded personal information, and must receive specific reasons for **adverse underwriting decisions**. Investigative consumer reports require disclosure, and the applicant may request an interview as part of the report. (The state citation is in the overlay; federal GLBA privacy rules also apply nationally.)

## Part 3: Federal Regulation (Outline 1.3)

### Fair Credit Reporting Act (15 USC 1681)
Governs consumer reports used in underwriting. Applicants must be told a report may be requested; if an **investigative consumer report** (interviews with neighbors, associates) is ordered, the applicant must be notified and may request disclosure of its nature and scope. If insurance is declined or rated up based on a report (**adverse action**), the insurer must tell the applicant and identify the reporting agency. Consumers may dispute and correct inaccurate information.

### Federal fraud and false statements — 18 USC 1033 & 1034
- It is a **federal crime** for anyone engaged in the business of insurance to make false statements or reports, embezzle, or willfully overvalue assets in financial documents.
- A person convicted of a **felony involving dishonesty or breach of trust** is **prohibited** from working in the business of insurance unless they obtain **written consent** from an insurance regulator — the **"1033 waiver."** The home-state consent question appears right on the NAIC license application background questions.
- 18 USC 1034 provides civil penalties and injunctive authority for 1033 violations.

## Drill card (universal)
- License trigger phrase: **sell, solicit, or negotiate**. NAIC lines: Life; A&H; Property; Casualty; Variable; Personal Lines; Credit; Travel.
- License kept by paying renewal + completing **CE** (hours/ethics/cycle = state-set). Lapsed license reinstatable within a state window without re-exam, with a penalty.
- Temporary license: **no exam**, limited period, for death/disability/military/public-interest, possibly with a **sponsor**.
- **~14 disciplinary grounds** from the NAIC model (dishonesty, law violations, fiduciary breaches, compliance failures); exact enumeration/fines = state.
- Appointments and terminations require regulator notice within state-set windows; for-cause terminations go to the producer by certified mail.
- Unfair Trade Practices: misrepresentation, defamation, coercion/boycott, false statements, no complaint record, unfair discrimination, **rebating** (give AND receive).
- Unfair claims settlement practices = the frequency/business-practice list.
- Privacy: notice of information practices, access/correction, reasons for adverse underwriting; investigative-report disclosure.
- Federal: **FCRA** (adverse action notice + reporting agency) and **18 USC 1033/1034** (felony-dishonesty bar + 1033 waiver).

## Exam traps
- *Production quotas are NOT a disciplinary ground* — the grounds cover dishonesty, legal violations, fiduciary breaches, and compliance failures, not sales performance.
- *Rebating is prohibited for both the giver and the receiver* (in states that retain the ban).
- *Unfair claims settlement violations require frequency* indicating a business practice — a single mistake is not the offense.
- *The 1033 waiver is required of anyone with a felony involving dishonesty/breach of trust, regardless of state licensing approval.*
- *The Commissioner enforces, the legislature writes* — the regulator issues regulations and holds hearings under the statutes but does not enact them.
