# A&H Producer Exam — National Core
## Module 5: Disability Income and Related Insurance
**Scope: national (applies to all states) | ~95% universal; state minimum-benefit/definition standards live in each state's overlay | Source: universal DI concepts + Social Security Act (federal)**

> **How to study this module:** Almost entirely universal content, and weight rose from 5% to 8% in the 2018 outline — Prometric wants more of it. The exam clusters around three things: the definitions of disability (own-occ vs. any-occ vs. Social Security's brutal standard), the timeline vocabulary (elimination vs. benefit periods), and which business DI product fits which scenario.

---

## Part 1: Qualifying for Benefits (Outline 5.1)

### Definitions of disability — most to least favorable to the insured
- **Own occupation:** unable to perform the duties of **your own** occupation. A surgeon who can no longer operate is disabled even if she could teach. Most generous, most expensive; often limited to an initial period (e.g., first 2 years) before converting to any-occ.
- **Any occupation:** unable to perform the duties of **any occupation for which you are reasonably suited by education, training, and experience**. Stricter. (Some states set minimum definition-of-disability standards by regulation; see the state overlay.)
- **Pure loss of income (income replacement):** benefits keyed to lost earnings rather than ability to work.
- **Presumptive disability:** specified losses — sight in both eyes, hearing, speech, or use of two limbs — are *presumed* totally disabling: full benefits, often with the elimination period waived, even if the insured can still work.
- **Physician care requirement:** the insured must generally be under a physician's regular care (waived when further care would be of no benefit).

## Part 2: Individual Disability Income Policies (Outline 5.2)

### The basic total disability plan
- **Income benefits (monthly indemnity):** a flat monthly amount — not tied to medical bills.
- **Elimination period:** the **waiting period** between disability onset and benefit start — a "time deductible" (30/90/180 days). Longer elimination = lower premium. No benefits accrue during it, and remember benefits are paid in arrears — a 90-day elimination means the first check arrives around day 120.
- **Benefit period:** how long payments continue (2 years, 5 years, to age 65). Longer = costlier.
- **Waiver of premium:** after a stated period of total disability (commonly 90 days), premiums are waived while disability continues — usually with refund of premiums paid during that qualifying period.

### Riders and coordination
- **Additional Monthly Benefit (AMB)** and **Social Insurance Supplement (SIS):** extra benefits during the months before Social Security disability kicks in — or while it's denied. SIS reduces dollar-for-dollar once social insurance pays.
- **Occupational vs. nonoccupational:** nonoccupational policies exclude on-the-job disabilities (those belong to workers comp); occupational covers 24 hours.
- **Partial disability benefit:** flat percentage (typically 50%) of the total benefit for a limited time when the insured can perform some but not all duties.
- **Residual disability benefit:** pays based on **percentage of earnings lost** — the modern, proportionate version of partial. A 40% income drop pays 40% of the benefit.
- **COLA rider:** inflation-adjusts benefits *during* a claim.
- **Future Increase Option (FIO) / Guaranteed insurability:** buy more coverage later **without medical underwriting** (financial underwriting still applies).
- **Relation of earnings to insurance:** ties maximum benefits to actual earnings (Module 4's omitted optional provision lives here in the outline).
- **Other cash benefits:** AD&D riders, rehabilitation benefit (retraining costs), medical reimbursement for nondisabling injuries.
- **Refund provisions:** return of premium and cash surrender value riders — expensive ways to get premiums back.
- **Common exclusions:** war, intentionally self-inflicted injury, normal pregnancy (older forms), committing a felony.

## Part 3: Underwriting and Group DI (Outlines 5.3–5.4)

- **Occupation drives everything:** classification by duties (not title) sets rates and benefit caps.
- **Benefit limits:** insurers cap benefits around **60–70% of earned income** — never 100%, to preserve the financial incentive to return to work.
- **Issuance alternatives** for marginal risks: rated premium, longer elimination period, shorter benefit period, exclusion rider.
- **Group DI:** **Short-Term Disability (STD)** — weekly benefit, brief elimination (0–14 days), benefit period weeks-to-a-year. **Long-Term Disability (LTD)** — monthly benefit after a long elimination (90–180 days), benefit period years-to-age-65, typically 60% of salary, coordinated with Social Security.

## Part 4: Business Disability Insurance (Outline 5.5)

| Product | Who's protected | What it pays |
|---|---|---|
| **Key person DI** | The business | Replaces profits/covers replacement cost while a key employee is disabled. Business owns, pays, receives. |
| **Disability buy-sell** | The other owners | Funds the purchase of a totally disabled owner's interest, per the buy-sell agreement — usually a lump sum after a long elimination period (1–2 years). |
| **Business overhead expense (BOE)** | The firm's bills | Reimburses **overhead** — rent, utilities, employee salaries — while the owner is disabled. Does **NOT** replace the owner's income. |
| **Disability reducing term** | A business loan | Pays the declining loan obligation if the borrowing owner is disabled. |

## Part 5: Social Security Disability and Workers Comp (Outlines 5.6–5.7)

### Social Security disability — the strictest definition in the business
- **Definition:** unable to engage in **any substantial gainful activity** due to a physical/mental impairment **expected to last at least 12 months or result in death**.
- **Waiting period: 5 months** — benefits begin with the **6th month** of disability.
- Qualification requires sufficient work credits (fully/currently insured status); benefits also flow to eligible dependents; converts to retirement benefits at full retirement age.

### Workers compensation (governed by each state's workers comp act — detail tested on the P&C side)
- Covers **occupational** injury and disease; benefits include medical care, disability income (a fraction of average weekly wage), rehabilitation, and death benefits — on a **no-fault** basis, as the employee's exclusive remedy against the employer.

## Practice Questions

**1.** Dr. Okafor, an orthopedic surgeon, develops a hand tremor ending her surgical career; she takes a teaching post at half her former income. Under an **own occupation** policy she is:
A) Not disabled — she is employed  B) Totally disabled — she cannot perform her own occupation's duties  C) Partially disabled only  D) Subject to the any-occupation test immediately
> **B.** Own-occ measures against the insured's own specialty; other employment doesn't defeat the claim. (Outline 5.1)

**2.** A disability policy has a 90-day elimination period. The insured is disabled January 1. Benefits are payable for the period beginning:
A) January 1  B) April 1 (approximately day 91)  C) July 1  D) After Social Security approves
> **B.** The elimination period is a time deductible; benefits accrue from roughly day 91 (and the first check, paid in arrears, arrives about a month later). (Outline 5.2)

**3.** An insured loses sight in both eyes but continues working as a consultant. Under the presumptive disability provision, the policy:
A) Pays nothing while he works  B) Pays the full total-disability benefit regardless of his ability to work  C) Pays the residual benefit  D) Pays only medical reimbursement
> **B.** Presumptive losses (sight, hearing, speech, two limbs) are deemed totally disabling; full benefits are paid even if the insured works. (Outline 5.1)

**4.** The DI product that reimburses a disabled dentist's office rent and staff salaries — but not her own lost income — is:
A) Key person disability  B) Business overhead expense  C) Disability buy-sell  D) Residual disability rider
> **B.** BOE keeps the practice's lights on; the owner's income needs her own individual DI policy. (Outline 5.5)

**5.** To qualify for Social Security disability benefits, a worker must be unable to engage in any substantial gainful activity due to an impairment expected to:
A) Last 6 months  B) Last at least 12 months or result in death  C) Be permanent  D) Prevent the worker's own occupation only
> **B.** The SSA test: any substantial gainful activity, 12-month duration or death — followed by a 5-month waiting period. (Outline 5.6)

**6.** The rider that lets an insured buy additional disability coverage later without proving medical insurability is the:
A) COLA rider  B) Future increase option  C) SIS rider  D) Return of premium rider
> **B.** The FIO (guaranteed insurability) rider locks in future purchase rights at standard rates regardless of health changes. (Outline 5.2)
