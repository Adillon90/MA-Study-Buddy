# A&H Producer Exam — National Core
## Module 4: Individual Accident & Sickness Policy Provisions
**Scope: national (applies to all states) | These are the NAIC Uniform Individual Accident and Sickness Policy Provision Law — adopted with the same values in every state; each state codifies it in its own code (MA: M.G.L. c. 175 § 108; CT: C.G.S. 38a-483). State-specific add-ons (prompt-pay, mandates) live in each state's overlay.**

> **How to study this module:** Every state adopted the NAIC Uniform Individual Accident and Sickness Policy Provision Law, so these numbers are national. The exam tests two buckets: **12 required provisions** (must appear in every individual A&S policy) and **optional provisions** (insurers may include them, but only in approved wording). Memorize the numbers in bold — they are the exam answers.

## Part 1: The 12 Required Provisions (NAIC Uniform Law)

Every individual accident and sickness policy must contain these provisions (or wording the Commissioner approves as no less favorable to the insured).

### 1. Entire Contract; Changes
The policy, endorsements, and attached papers make up the entire contract. No change is valid unless approved by an **executive officer** of the insurer and endorsed on or attached to the policy. **Agents cannot change the policy or waive any provision.**
- *Exam trap:* A producer telling a client "don't worry, that exclusion won't apply to you" has no legal effect.

### 2. Time Limit on Certain Defenses (Incontestability)
After **2 years** from the issue date, only **fraudulent** misstatements in the application can be used to void the policy or deny a claim. Claims for losses starting after the 2-year mark cannot be denied because a condition existed before coverage, unless that condition is excluded by name or specific description.
- *Exam focus:* 2 years. Fraud is the exception for standard policies; a guaranteed renewable policy may use an "Incontestable" version that drops even the fraud exception.

### 3. Grace Period
The premium grace period depends on how often premiums are paid:
- **7 days** — weekly premium policies
- **10 days** — monthly premium policies
- **31 days** — all other premium modes
Coverage continues in force during the grace period.
- *State note:* states often require advance nonrenewal notice when the insurer reserves the right to refuse renewal (the day-count is state-specific — see overlay).

### 4. Reinstatement
If a lapsed policy's premium is later accepted **without** requiring a reinstatement application, the policy is automatically reinstated. If the insurer requires an application and issues a conditional receipt, the policy is reinstated when the application is approved — or automatically on the **45th day** after the conditional receipt, unless the insurer has disapproved it in writing.
A reinstated policy covers:
- **Accidents — immediately** after reinstatement
- **Sickness — only if it begins more than 10 days** after reinstatement
Premiums accepted at reinstatement can be applied no further back than **60 days**.

### 5. Notice of Claim
Written notice of claim must go to the insurer within **20 days** of the loss, or as soon as reasonably possible. For disability income benefits payable for at least 2 years, the insurer may require proof of continuing disability every **6 months**.

### 6. Claim Forms
The insurer must furnish claim forms within **15 days** of receiving notice of claim. If it doesn't, the claimant satisfies proof-of-loss requirements by submitting written proof of the occurrence, character, and extent of loss.

### 7. Proof of Loss
Written proof of loss is due within **90 days** of the loss (or, for continuing periodic payments, within 90 days after the end of the period the insurer is liable for). Late proof is excused if it wasn't reasonably possible to file on time — but with a hard outer limit of **1 year**, unless the claimant lacks legal capacity.

### 8. Time of Payment of Claims
Non-periodic claims are paid **immediately** upon receipt of written proof of loss. Periodic benefits (like disability income) must be paid at least **monthly**.

### 9. Payment of Claims
Death proceeds go to the named beneficiary; if none is in effect, to the insured's **estate**. All other benefits are payable to the insured. An optional "facility of payment" clause lets the insurer pay up to **$1,000** to a blood relative or relative by marriage who is equitably entitled, when the payee is the estate, a minor, or someone not competent to give a valid release.

### 10. Physical Examinations
The insurer, **at its own expense**, may examine the insured as often as reasonably required while a claim is pending.

### 11. Legal Actions
No lawsuit on the policy may begin sooner than **60 days** after proof of loss is filed, and none may begin more than **3 years** after proof of loss was required.

### 12. Change of Beneficiary
The insured may change the beneficiary at any time unless the designation is **irrevocable**. Beneficiary consent is not required to change beneficiaries, surrender, or assign the policy.

### 13. Right to Examine (Free Look)
The current exam outline lists the free look alongside the required provisions. The owner may cancel within a set number of days (commonly **10 days**) of delivery for any reason; surrendering the policy voids it **from the beginning** with a **full premium refund**. (The exact free-look period is state-specific — see each state's overlay.)
- *Exam focus:* full refund, void from inception (not from the cancellation date).

## Part 1B: Other General Provisions (Outline Section 4.3)

These aren't in the mandatory list but are tested in the same exam section:

- **Insuring clause** — the insurer's basic promise: who is covered, against what perils, for what period.
- **Consideration clause** — the exchange of value: the applicant's statements in the application plus the first premium, in return for the insurer's promise to pay.
- **Renewability clause** — the hierarchy candidates must rank from most to least favorable to the insured:
  1. **Noncancelable** — insurer can never cancel, refuse renewal, or raise premiums (rates guaranteed)
  2. **Guaranteed renewable** — insurer must renew, but may raise premiums by class
  3. **Conditionally renewable** — insurer may decline renewal only for reasons stated in the policy
  4. **Renewable at the option of the insurer** — insurer may decline renewal for any reason at renewal
- **Military suspense provision** — suspends coverage while the insured is on active military duty, with premium refunded or coverage restored after service.

## Part 2: Key Optional Provisions (NAIC Uniform Law optional provisions)

Insurers may include these, but only in statutory or Commissioner-approved wording:

| Provision | What it does |
|---|---|
| Change of Occupation | More hazardous job → benefits reduced to what the premium would buy at the riskier rate. Less hazardous job → premium reduced and excess returned pro rata. |
| Misstatement of Age | Benefits adjusted to what the premium would have purchased at the correct age. |
| Other Insurance in This Insurer | Limits stacking of multiple policies from the same insurer; excess coverage is void and excess premium refunded. |
| Insurance with Other Insurers | Pro-rates expense-incurred benefits among insurers when the insurer wasn't notified of other coverage. |
| Unpaid Premium | Any premium due may be deducted from a claim payment. |
| Conformity with State Statutes | Conflicting policy provisions are automatically amended to meet state minimums. |
| Illegal Occupation | No liability where a contributing cause of loss was the insured committing/attempting a felony or working in an illegal occupation. |

*Scope note: the current outline lists optional provisions (1), (2), (3), (4), (5), (7), (9), and (10) — it omits the statute's overinsurance provision (6). The related concept, "relation of earnings to insurance," is instead tested under outline Section 5.0 (Disability Income), where benefits are limited to a percentage of earned income but never reduced below a combined $300/month.*

## Practice Questions

**1.** Tom pays his individual health insurance premium monthly. How long is his grace period?
A) 7 days  B) 10 days  C) 31 days  D) 60 days
> **B.** Monthly premium policies get a 10-day grace period; weekly get 7, all others get 31. (NAIC Uniform Law grace provision)

**2.** Maria's lapsed policy was reinstated on June 1 after she submitted an application and conditional receipt. On June 5 she gets sick. Is her illness covered?
A) Yes, coverage is immediate  B) No — sickness must begin more than 10 days after reinstatement  C) Yes, but only at 50% benefits  D) No, reinstated policies exclude all sickness for 90 days
> **B.** Reinstated policies cover accidents immediately but sickness only if it begins more than 10 days after reinstatement.

**3.** A claimant submits written proof of loss but the insurer furnishes no claim forms within 15 days of the notice of claim. The claimant may:
A) Not file until forms arrive  B) Satisfy proof-of-loss by submitting written proof of the occurrence, character, and extent of loss  C) Sue immediately  D) Collect double benefits
> **B.** If the insurer fails to furnish claim forms within 15 days, the claimant meets proof-of-loss by submitting written proof of the loss. (Claim Forms provision)

**4.** Three years after his policy was issued, an insurer discovers the insured unintentionally misstated his medical history on the application. Can it void the policy?
A) Yes, misstatements always void coverage  B) No — after 2 years, only fraudulent misstatements can void the policy  C) Yes, if it refunds premiums  D) Only with Commissioner approval
> **B.** The time limit on certain defenses bars non-fraudulent misstatement defenses after 2 years.

**5.** Which statement about agent authority is TRUE under the Entire Contract provision?
A) Agents may waive policy provisions verbally  B) Agents may amend policies with home-office notice  C) No agent has authority to change the policy or waive its provisions  D) Agents may change beneficiaries on the insured's behalf
> **C.** Only an executive officer can approve changes, and the approval must be endorsed on or attached to the policy.
