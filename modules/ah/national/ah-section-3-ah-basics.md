# A&H Producer Exam — National Core
## Module 3: Accident, Health & Sickness Insurance Basics
**Scope: national (applies to all states) | ~70% universal | Source: universal A&H doctrine + federal (FCRA, HSA/MSA). State-specific items (marketing/advertising rules, HIV/genetic underwriting limits, replacement notices, any state individual mandate) live in each state's overlay.**

> **How to study this module:** Half vocabulary, half producer conduct. The definitions, policy classes, limited policies, HSAs/MSAs, and underwriting sources are universal. State law layers on top in producer responsibilities (marketing/advertising rules, the guaranty-association gag rule, replacement notices) and underwriting limits on HIV and genetic information; some states (e.g., Massachusetts) also impose an individual coverage mandate. Those specifics are in each state's overlay.

## Part 1: Perils, Losses, and Policy Classes (Outlines 3.1–3.3)

### The two perils
- **Accidental injury** — bodily harm from an accident: sudden, unforeseen, unintended. Policies historically used either "accidental **means**" wording (the *cause* must be accidental — stricter) or the modern "accidental **bodily injury/results**" wording (the *result* must be accidental — broader, more favorable to the insured).
- **Sickness** — illness or disease first manifesting after the policy's effective date.

### The four principal loss/benefit types
1. **Loss of income from disability** — replaces wages (Module 5)
2. **Hospital/medical expense** — pays cost of care (Module 6)
3. **Dental/vision expense** (Module 8)
4. **Long-term care / home health expense** (Module 9)

### Classes of policies
- **Individual vs. group** — own policy and underwriting vs. certificate under a master contract
- **Private vs. government** — commercial/BCBS/HMO coverage vs. Medicare, Medicaid, workers' compensation, Social Security disability
- **Limited vs. comprehensive** — and a terminology twist this exam's outline uses: limited-benefit products are classified as **accident/sickness policies**, while comprehensive medical coverage is **health insurance**. The outline itself is organized around this distinction.

## Part 2: Limited Policies (Outline 3.4)

Limited policies pay only for **specifically defined perils or limited amounts**, and most states require a prominent **notice to the insured** that the coverage is limited (the exact notice wording is state-specific — see overlay). Types to recognize:
- **Accidental Death & Dismemberment (AD&D)** — pays the **principal sum** for accidental death (or both hands/feet/sight), **capital sum** (a fraction, often 50%) for lesser dismemberments
- **Specified (dread) disease** — one named disease, e.g., cancer-only
- **Hospital indemnity** — a fixed dollar amount **per day hospitalized**, paid regardless of actual expenses
- **Credit disability** — pays the insured's loan payments during disability; the creditor is beneficiary
- **Blanket insurance** — covers a changing group (sports team members, bus passengers, students) without naming individuals
- Prescription drug, dental-only, vision-only, and Medicare supplement policies

## Part 3: Government Plans, MSAs, and HSAs (Outline 3.5)

- **Government plans** (Medicare, Medicaid, workers' comp) are excluded from private-policy duplication — covered elsewhere in the course.
- **Archer Medical Savings Accounts (MSAs):** the older vehicle — limited to **self-employed individuals and employees of small employers (50 or fewer)** paired with a high-deductible plan; contributions capped at a percentage of the deductible. Largely closed to new participants.
- **Health Savings Accounts (HSAs):** the current vehicle — requires enrollment in a qualified **high-deductible health plan (HDHP)**, no other disqualifying coverage, not enrolled in Medicare, not claimed as a dependent. The famous **triple tax advantage**: contributions deductible, growth tax-deferred, withdrawals for qualified medical expenses tax-free. Funds roll over year to year (no use-it-or-lose-it) and are portable.
- **State child-health safety nets:** many states run a primary/preventive coverage program for uninsured children — a state safety-net program, not private insurance (the specific program is named in the state overlay).

## Part 4: Producer Responsibilities (Outline 3.6)

- **Marketing and advertising rules:** advertisements must be truthful and not misleading; the commissioner enforces form, content, and disclosure standards (each state's citation in its overlay).
- **Guaranty Association gag rule:** the Life and Health Insurance Guaranty Association protects policyholders if an insurer becomes insolvent — but producers are **prohibited from using its existence as a sales tool** ("don't worry, the state guarantees it"). Classic exam question.
- **Outline of coverage:** a plain-language summary of benefits, exclusions, and renewability, delivered to the applicant at application (or with the policy).
- **Disclosure / privacy:** the producer follows the state's information-practices and privacy framework when handling applicant data.
- **Field underwriting:** the producer is the insurer's first line of risk selection — accurate, complete applications; reporting observed risk factors via the **producer report**; collecting premium; obtaining signatures.
- **Delivery requirements:** deliver the policy promptly; if no premium was collected with the application, obtain a **statement of continued good health** plus the premium for coverage to take effect.
- **Errors & omissions exposure:** the common producer failures — misstating coverage, failing to deliver, failing to forward premium, exceeding authority.

## Part 5: Individual Underwriting by the Insurer (Outline 3.7)

### Sources of underwriting information
- **The application** — the primary source; the applicant's statements are **representations** (not warranties)
- **Producer report** — the agent's observations; not seen by the applicant
- **Attending Physician Statement (APS)** — detail from the applicant's own doctor
- **Investigative consumer report** — interviews about character/lifestyle; FCRA notice rights apply (Module 1)
- **Medical Information Bureau (MIB)** — a nonprofit **member-insurer bureau** of coded prior-application data used to deter fraud and misrepresentation. The applicant must be notified and authorize; an insurer may **not decline solely** on MIB data.
- **Medical exams and lab tests, including HIV:** HIV testing requires the applicant's **written informed consent** on an approved form, with confidentiality protections; underwriting may not use **sexual orientation** in risk classification. (Many states regulate this; exact citation in the state overlay.)
- **Genetic information:** many states bar insurers from requiring **genetic tests** or using genetic information in accident & sickness underwriting without consent (a strong consumer protection; state citation in the overlay).

### Risk classification
- **Preferred** — better than average; lowest rates
- **Standard** — average; normal rates
- **Substandard** — higher risk; issued with a rated premium, exclusion rider, or modified benefits — or declined

## Part 6: Replacement (Outline 3.8)

Replacing existing accident & sickness coverage deserves caution because the client can be hurt: new waiting periods, benefits lost, exclusions gained, and underwriting at an older age. The standard rules across states: the **application must contain a question** asking whether the new policy replaces existing A&S coverage; when replacement is involved, the agent or carrier furnishes a prescribed **"Notice to Applicant Regarding Replacement"** **at application or before the policy is issued**, leaving a copy with the applicant and keeping a **signed copy** in the carrier's records; the notice warns that **existing conditions may not be covered** under the new policy and urges comparison before terminating the old coverage. A bad replacement is a textbook E&O scenario. (State-specific replacement forms/citations, and any state **individual coverage mandate**, live in the state overlay.)

## Practice Questions

**1.** A policy paying $200 for each day the insured is hospitalized, regardless of actual charges, is a:
A) Major medical plan  B) Hospital indemnity policy  C) Disability income policy  D) Dread disease policy
> **B.** Hospital indemnity pays a fixed per-day amount independent of expenses incurred — a limited policy that requires a prominent limited-coverage notice. (Outline 3.4)

**2.** During a sales presentation, a producer reassures a nervous client: "Even if this insurer fails, the state Guaranty Association will pay your claims." The producer has:
A) Properly disclosed a consumer protection  B) Violated the rule prohibiting use of the Association as a sales inducement  C) Committed twisting  D) Done nothing wrong if the statement is true
> **B.** The Guaranty Association exists, but producers are barred from advertising or using it to induce a sale — even truthfully (the gag rule; state cite in overlay).

**3.** In states that regulate it, an insurer underwriting an individual health application may:
A) Require a genetic test as a condition of issue  B) Classify risk based on sexual orientation  C) Order an HIV test with the applicant's written informed consent  D) Decline solely on an MIB code
> **C.** HIV testing is permitted only with written informed consent; genetic-testing requirements are commonly barred; sexual orientation cannot be used; and MIB data alone cannot support a declination. (Exact state cites in the overlay.)

**4.** The producer takes an application but collects no premium. For coverage to begin at policy delivery, the producer must collect the premium AND obtain:
A) A new application  B) A statement of continued good health  C) An attending physician statement  D) A conditional receipt
> **B.** Without premium at application there is no interim coverage; at delivery the insurer requires payment plus a statement of continued good health. (Outline 3.6)

**5.** An HSA's "triple tax advantage" requires the account holder to be enrolled in:
A) Any health plan  B) A qualified high-deductible health plan (HDHP)  C) Medicare  D) A limited dread-disease policy
> **B.** HSA eligibility requires a qualified HDHP (and no disqualifying coverage); contributions are deductible, growth is tax-deferred, and qualified-medical withdrawals are tax-free. (Outline 3.5)

**6.** The Medical Information Bureau (MIB) is best described as:
A) A government registry of health claims  B) A nonprofit bureau of coded underwriting data shared by member insurers to deter misrepresentation  C) A credit reporting agency  D) The state's external review agency
> **B.** MIB is a member-insurer information exchange; applicants must authorize access, and adverse action can't rest on MIB codes alone. (Outline 3.7)

**7.** An applicant's statements on a health insurance application are legally considered:
A) Warranties — guaranteed true  B) Representations — true to the best of the applicant's knowledge  C) Concealments  D) Binding estimates
> **B.** Application statements are representations; only material misrepresentations affect recovery (ties to the § 108(5) rule from Module 4). (Outline 3.7)
