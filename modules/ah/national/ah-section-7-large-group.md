# A&H Producer Exam — National Core
## Module 7: Large Group Health Insurance
**Scope: national (applies to all states) | Source: universal group-insurance mechanics + federal law (COBRA, ERISA, the Civil Rights Act/Pregnancy Discrimination Act, ADEA, Medicare Secondary Payer, federal COB model). State continuation statutes ("mini-COBRA"), small-group rating rules, and state nondiscrimination provisions live in the per-state overlay (e.g., `ah/MA/ah-MA-section-7-large-group.md`)**

> **How to study this module:** Two halves. This national core is the universal half: how group insurance works (master contract, certificates, rating, eligibility, coordination of benefits) and the federal continuation/regulation layer (COBRA durations and premiums, COB birthday rule, Medicare secondary payer, pregnancy discrimination). The state overlay carries the **mini-COBRA / state continuation statutes** — extra continuation rights, each with its own trigger and clock — plus the state's small-group rating rules. Master the COBRA durations and the COB birthday rule here; the state continuation clocks are the overlay's job.

## Part 1: Characteristics of Group Insurance (Outline 7.1)

- **One master contract** is issued to the group sponsor (the employer/policyholder). Covered employees don't receive policies — they receive **certificates of coverage** summarizing their benefits. Employees are **certificate holders**, not policyowners.
- **No individual underwriting** — the group is underwritten as a unit, which is why group coverage is generally cheaper and available regardless of an individual's health.
- **Experience rating vs. community rating:** large groups are typically **experience rated** — premiums reflect the group's own claims history. **Community rating** prices everyone in a broad pool the same way; many states apply modified community rating to the individual/small-group market (see overlay).

## Part 2: Eligible Groups and Marketing (Outlines 7.2–7.3)

- Groups must exist **for a purpose other than buying insurance** — primarily **employment-related groups** (individual employer groups in this exam's scope).
- **Regulatory jurisdiction:** a group plan is governed by the law of the state where the **master policy is delivered** — which is why the outline stresses "place of delivery." States often extend their mandates to out-of-state group policies that cover their residents (see overlay).
- Group advertising is subject to the same unfair-practice rules as individual marketing (Module 1).

## Part 3: Employer Group Underwriting and Eligibility (Outline 7.4)

### What the insurer underwrites (since it can't underwrite individuals)
The **group's characteristics**: size, industry, age/sex mix, turnover (persistency), plan design (cost-sharing levels), prior claims experience, and the employer's **administrative capability** to handle enrollment and premium collection.

### Employee and dependent eligibility
Typical eligibility requires being a **full-time, actively-at-work employee**; new hires may face a probationary period, then enroll during their eligibility window or wait for the **annual open enrollment**. Dependent eligibility follows the rules from Module 6 — children to age 26, newborns from the moment of birth, adopted children from petition/placement.

### Coordination of Benefits (COB) — federal/NAIC model
When a person is covered by two group plans, COB rules decide payment order so combined benefits never exceed 100% of the expense:
- A plan covering the person **as an employee pays primary**; the plan covering them as a dependent pays secondary.
- For dependent children of married parents, the **birthday rule** applies: the plan of the parent whose **birthday falls earlier in the calendar year** is primary (not the older parent — the earlier birthday).
- For divorced/separated parents, a court decree controls; otherwise the custodial parent's plan generally pays first.

### Changing insurance companies — no-loss/no-gain
When an employer switches carriers, employees should neither lose nor gain by the changeover: the new plan picks up ongoing claims without imposing fresh probationary periods (**no-loss no-gain**), and **deductible and coinsurance credits carry over** for amounts already satisfied that year.

### Extension of benefits
An employee who is **totally disabled** when the group coverage terminates continues to receive benefits **for that disabling condition** for a limited extension period — the disability doesn't lose coverage just because the plan ended. (The duration is plan-form-dependent; teach it as a concept.)

## Part 4: Federal Continuation — COBRA

### Federal COBRA (employers with 20+ employees)
Qualified beneficiaries may continue the group coverage **at their own cost** after a qualifying event:
- **18 months** — employee's termination (other than gross misconduct) or reduction in hours
- **29 months** — the 18-month event where the beneficiary is **disabled** (Social Security definition) during the first 60 days of continuation
- **36 months** — employee's death, divorce/legal separation, employee's Medicare entitlement, or a child aging out of dependent status
- **Election period: 60 days** from the later of the qualifying event or the COBRA notice
- Premium: up to **102%** of the group rate (up to **150%** during the disability-extension months)

> **State continuation ("mini-COBRA"):** Most states layer their own continuation statutes on top of COBRA — typically extending COBRA-style rights to **small groups below the 20-employee federal threshold**, and sometimes adding extra triggers (layoff, divorce, death of the insured) with their own clocks. These vary substantially by state and are the overlay's job. *Exam tip:* know that the federal floor is COBRA at 20+, and that your state's statute fills the gap below that and may add events.

## Part 5: Small Employer and Individual Medical Plans (Outline 7.5)

- **Small employer:** federal ACA defines a small employer as **1–50 employees** (states may use 1–100). Many states **merge** the individual and small-group markets into one risk pool — the exact definition and pooling are state-specific (see overlay).
- **Guaranteed availability and modified community rating (ACA):** carriers must offer plans to all eligible small groups, and rating is limited to a short list of factors — **age (capped 3:1 federally), geographic area, tobacco use, and family size** — with **health status and claims experience prohibited** as rating factors. States may narrow these further (e.g., a tighter age band or tobacco prohibition — see overlay).
- **Guaranteed renewability:** small-group plans renew at the employer's option except for statutory causes (nonpayment, fraud, market withdrawal).

## Part 6: Federal Regulation of Employer Plans (Outline 7.6)

- **ERISA:** federal law governing private-employer benefit plans — fiduciary duties, reporting and disclosure (SPD), and claims-procedure rules. Self-funded ERISA plans are generally **exempt from state insurance mandates** (preemption), which is why some employer plans don't carry state-mandated benefits.
- **Civil Rights Act / Pregnancy Discrimination Act:** employers with **15+ employees** must treat pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions the same as any other medical condition — same benefits, same cost-sharing.
- **ADEA (Age Discrimination in Employment Act):** employers with **20+ employees** may not use age to deny benefits to older workers; active employees 65+ keep the same coverage offered to younger workers.
- **Relationship with Medicare (Medicare Secondary Payer):** for active employees 65+ in groups of **20+ employees**, the **group plan pays primary and Medicare is secondary**. Employers may offer Medicare **carve-outs or supplements** for retirees, where Medicare pays primary and the employer plan fills gaps.

## Drill card (universal)
- **1 master contract / many certificates;** employees are certificate holders, not policyowners.
- Group underwritten as a unit (no individual underwriting); large groups **experience rated**.
- COB: employee-coverage pays **primary**; **birthday rule** = parent whose birthday is **earlier in the calendar year** (not the older parent).
- No-loss/no-gain on a carrier switch; deductible credits carry over.
- **COBRA: 18 / 29 / 36 months; 60-day election; 102% (150% disability).** Threshold **20+ employees**.
- State mini-COBRA fills below 20 and may add triggers (overlay).
- ACA small group **1–50**; rating factors age/area/tobacco/family only — **no health status or claims experience**.
- ERISA preempts state mandates for **self-funded** plans; PDA at **15+**; ADEA at **20+**; MSP makes the group plan primary for active 65+ in 20+ groups.

## Exam traps
- *The COB birthday rule looks at whose birthday is earlier **in the year**, not which parent is older.*
- *COBRA durations: 18 = termination/reduced hours; 36 = death/divorce/Medicare/aging-out; 29 = the disability extension.* A carrier change is **not** a qualifying event.
- *COBRA's 20-employee floor* — smaller groups rely on **state** continuation (overlay), not COBRA.
- *Self-funded ERISA plans escape state-mandated benefits* via preemption — a reason a group plan may lack a state mandate.
- *Pregnancy must be treated like any other medical condition* (PDA, 15+ employees) — not as an excludable condition.
