# Life Producer Exam — National Core
## Module L3: Life Insurance Basics
**Scope: national (applies to all states) | Maps to outline Section 3.0 — 20% (20 of 100 items), the largest single-topic build | Source: verified study notes (RN/KW Legal Concepts, Uses of Life Insurance, Underwriting and Policy Issue, green/must-know) | MA touchpoints flagged inline; contract characteristics (3.x) also covered in reused A&H §2.0**

> **How to study this module:** This is the broadest section. It runs from why someone buys life insurance (insurable interest, personal and business uses) through how the insurer decides to issue it (underwriting, risk classes) to how the producer puts it on the books (field underwriting, receipts, delivery). The heaviest exam payload is in **business uses** (buy-sell structures) and **underwriting sources + receipts**. Keep the buy-sell "who owns/pays/benefits" grids straight, and keep conditional vs. binding receipts apart.

---

## Part 1: Insurable interest (Outline 3.1)

- For **life insurance, insurable interest must exist at the time of application (policy inception)** between the policyowner and the insured. It does **not** need to exist at the time of death (unlike property insurance).
- Insurable interest exists when the insured's death would cause the policyowner a **clear financial loss** (or out of love and affection, for close family). Examples: yourself, a spouse, a business partner, a key employee, a debtor.

## Part 2: Personal uses and determining the amount (Outline 3.2, 3.4)

- **Personal uses:** survivor protection, estate creation, cash accumulation, security, estate conservation (cash to pay estate taxes so heirs keep the assets).
- **Human life value approach** — estimates the **dollar value of future earnings** lost if the breadwinner dies; sets the face to replace that economic value.
- **Needs approach** — adds up what the family actually needs (final expenses, mortgage/housing, education, ongoing income, emergency fund, retirement) minus existing resources. Best for answering **"how much should this client apply for."**

## Part 3: Business uses (Outline 3.5)

**Buy-sell (business continuation) agreements** fund the orderly transfer of an owner's interest at death/disability. The structures and the "who owns, pays, and is beneficiary" answer are heavily tested:

- **Sole proprietor** — a key employee/successor owns, pays for, and is beneficiary of a policy on the proprietor; uses the proceeds to buy the business from the estate.
- **Partnership — cross-purchase** — **each partner** owns, pays, and is beneficiary of a policy on **each other partner**. Best with **few** partners (number of policies grows fast).
- **Partnership — entity** — the **partnership itself** owns, pays, and is beneficiary of a policy on each partner. Best with **several** partners.
- **Close corporation — cross-purchase** — surviving **stockholders** buy the deceased's shares (each owns policies on the others; the corporation is not a party).
- **Close corporation — stock redemption** (= the entity plan for a corporation) — the **corporation** owns, pays, and is beneficiary, then redeems the deceased stockholder's shares.
- **Key person insurance** — the **business** owns, pays for, and is beneficiary of a policy on a key owner/officer/employee; covers the cost of lost production and finding/training a replacement. **Premiums are not tax-deductible; death proceeds to the business are not taxable.**

**Executive benefit plans:**
- **Deferred compensation** — pays a highly paid employee later (at retirement/disability/death); employee defers their own income.
- **Salary continuation** — same idea but **employer-funded**.
- **Split-dollar** — employer and employee **share the cost** of a policy on the employee; a method of buying insurance, not a policy type.

## Part 4: Classes of life insurance (Outline 3.6)

Quick classification axes (detail lives in §4.0 Policies): **group vs. individual**, **permanent vs. term**, **ordinary vs. industrial (home service)**, **participating (dividends) vs. nonparticipating**, **fixed vs. variable**. Variable products are **securities** requiring a FINRA registration plus the life license; MA regulates them under **211 CMR 95**.

## Part 5: Premiums (Outline 3.7)

- **Three primary premium factors:** **mortality** (expected death claims, from mortality tables), **interest** (assumed earnings on reserves — higher assumed interest lowers premium), and **expense** (the insurer's cost of doing business, the "loading").
- **Payment mode** — more frequent payment (monthly vs. annual) means a **higher total annual cost** because of lost interest and added billing expense.
- *Pending verification:* the precise **net single premium vs. gross annual premium** mechanics aren't in the green source notes; pull from a premiums source before writing questions on them.

## Part 6: Field underwriting and producer responsibilities (Outline 3.8)

- **Field underwriting** is done by the **agent** (face-to-face), the insurer's first defense against **adverse selection**: complete the application correctly, collect the initial premium, report suspected misstatements, and forward promptly.
- **Application errors** — the agent corrects and has the **applicant initial** the change; an agent may **never** alter the application without the applicant present. Incomplete applications are returned.
- **At delivery**, the producer must hand over the **Buyer's Guide** (general descriptions of policy types, for comparison shopping) and the **Policy Summary** (specifics of the policy bought — premium, benefits). A **suitability** form confirms the product fits the client.
- **Signatures** — agent and applicant sign; if the applicant differs from the insured (other than a parent insuring a minor), the **insured also signs** (third-party ownership).
- **Backdating** — predating the application up to **6 months** ("saving age") to get a lower premium based on a younger age; the owner pays the back premiums. (MA cites **175:130**.)

## Part 7: Premium receipts and effective date (Outline 3.8)

- **Conditional receipt** (the common one in life) — given when the application **and** initial premium are collected. Coverage takes effect from the **application date or the medical exam date, whichever is later**, *provided* the applicant proves insurable as applied for.
- **Binding receipt** — coverage from the application date **regardless of insurability**, for ~30–60 days until the insurer accepts or rejects; rare in life (common in auto/home).
- **No initial premium with the application** → the policy is not effective until the premium is collected **and** the insured signs a **statement of continued good health** at delivery.
- **Constructive delivery** — the insurer relinquishes control of the policy (e.g., mails it to the agent for unconditional delivery); it counts as delivered even if the agent never physically hands it over — **unless** the company conditioned delivery on good health.

## Part 8: Individual underwriting sources (Outline 3.9)

- **The application** is the **primary** source. **Part I** = general/personal info; **Part II** = medical history; **Part III** = the **agent's report** (the agent's observations, not shown to the applicant).
- **Medical report / paramedical exam** — required above a face threshold (e.g., $50,000–$100,000); blood/urine may be required.
- **Attending Physician's Statement (APS)** — pulled when the medical section flags something needing the treating doctor's records.
- **Medical Information Bureau (MIB)** — nonprofit member exchange of coded prior-application data that helps detect misrepresentation and fight adverse selection. An insurer **may not** decline solely on MIB data; **MIB is not notified when an application is declined**; MIB data may be released to the proposed insured's **physician**.
- **Inspection / investigative consumer report** — character, lifestyle, finances; the applicant has the **right to a copy** and, under the **Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA, 1970)**, must be **notified in writing** when a credit/consumer report is used or when a rate is increased because of it.
- **Special questionnaires** — for aviation, military, hazardous occupation/hobby.
- **HIV consent** — even when a blood test is routine, the producer must obtain a **signed HIV consent form**. (MA: **211 CMR 36.03**; insurers may not use sexual orientation in classification.)
- **USA PATRIOT Act** — requires insurer anti-money-laundering programs (cash-value policies are a laundering risk).

## Part 9: Risk classification (Outline 3.9)

- **Preferred** — better than average (nonsmoker, good health/family history); **lowest** rates.
- **Standard** — average risk; normal rates.
- **Substandard (rated)** — higher risk (chronic conditions); issued with a **rated-up premium**, exclusion rider, or modified benefits.
- **Declined** — risk too high to insure (terminal illness, excessive impairments).

## Drill card (the testable facts)
- Life insurable interest must exist at **application/inception**, not at death.
- Human life value = replace future earnings; **needs approach** = how much to apply for.
- Buy-sell grids: sole prop (successor owns), partnership cross-purchase (each on each) vs. entity (partnership owns); close corp cross-purchase vs. **stock redemption** (corp owns). Key person: **business** owns/pays/benefits, premiums **not deductible**, proceeds **not taxable**.
- Three premium factors: **mortality, interest, expense**. More frequent mode = higher total cost.
- Field underwriter = the agent; correct errors only with the **applicant's initials**.
- Deliver **Buyer's Guide** (general) + **Policy Summary** (specific) at delivery.
- Conditional receipt = coverage from **application or exam, whichever later**, if insurable. Binding = covered regardless until rejection (rare in life).
- No premium up front → need premium + **statement of good health** at delivery.
- Backdating up to **6 months** to save age (MA 175:130).
- MIB: not notified on decline, can't decline solely on it, may go to the **physician**. FCRA: written notice + right to a copy.
- Risk classes: preferred / standard / substandard (rated) / declined.

## Exam traps
- *Insurable interest for life is tested at **inception only** — the classic contrast with property insurance (which needs it at the time of loss).*
- *Cross-purchase vs. entity/stock-redemption: who **owns and is beneficiary** — the individuals or the business — is the whole question.*
- *Conditional vs. binding receipt:* conditional ties coverage to **insurability** (application/exam date, whichever later); binding covers regardless until rejection.
- *Key person premiums are **not deductible**, but the death proceeds are **tax-free** to the business* — the same pattern as personally-owned DI.
- *An agent can never change an application alone; the **applicant must initial** every correction.*
- *MIB is an information exchange, not a decision-maker — an insurer cannot decline on MIB data alone and does not report declinations to it.*
