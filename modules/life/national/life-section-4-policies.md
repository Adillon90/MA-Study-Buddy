# Life Producer Exam — National Core
## Module L4: Types of Life Insurance Policies
**Scope: national (applies to all states) | Maps to outline Section 4.0 — 15% (15 of 100 items) | Source: verified study notes (KW/RN Life Insurance Policies, green/must-know items) | Layer 1 of the national-core + state-overlay build**

> **How to study this module:** This is the heart of the life exam. Almost every question is "which policy fits this need?" The exam tests whether you can keep the policy types apart: term vs. whole vs. universal vs. variable, and the special-use combinations. Anchor on the three things that define any policy: the **premium** (level/flexible/single), the **death benefit** (level/decreasing/increasing/flexible), and the **cash value** (none/guaranteed/non-guaranteed). Get those three straight for each type and the scenarios answer themselves.

---

## Part 1: Three ways to classify life insurance

- **Industrial life** — very small face amounts ($1,000–$2,000), premiums paid weekly to a debit agent. Designed for **burial coverage**.
- **Group life** — written for members of a group (employer, association, union) under one **master contract**. The group is underwritten as a whole, not member by member, so usually **no evidence of insurability** is required.
- **Ordinary life** — individual insurance, made up of **term** (temporary) and **whole life** (permanent).

## Part 2: Term insurance — pure protection, no cash value

Term is the **cheapest** type of pure life insurance. It pays a death benefit **only if the insured dies during the policy term**, has a **termination date**, and builds **no cash value**. For the same face amount, term is **always** cheaper than whole life. Think of it as **renting**.

- **Level (level-premium) term** — level face amount, level premium for the period. Premiums are higher than annual renewable term because they stay level, but they jump at each **renewal**. This is the answer for "coverage for a specified period at the lowest premium."
- **Decreasing term** — **level premium**, face amount **decreases** on a schedule. Used for **mortgage protection** and other declining debt. Expires when the debt is paid.
- **Increasing term** — face amount **increases** over time (by set amounts or a percentage of original face).
- **Annual renewable term (ART)** — level face that renews **annually** without proof of insurability; premium rises each year.

### Two privileges that lock in insurability
- **Renewable** — right to continue coverage after expiration **without proving insurability**. Premium rises to the insured's **attained (current) age**.
- **Convertible** — right to convert term into a **permanent** policy **without evidence of insurability** ("rent to own"). Premium is based on **attained age** at conversion.
- *Exam trap:* On either renewal or conversion, insurability is guaranteed but the **premium still goes up**, because the insurer uses the insured's **attained age**, not original age. The number-one driver of life premium cost is **attained/current age**.

- **Term rider** — covers additional family members (spouse, children) by attaching them to a main policy. A term rider is **always level term** and is cheaper than separate policies.

## Part 3: Whole life — permanent protection with living benefits

Whole life provides a death benefit for the insured's **entire life**, **matures at age 100**, normally has a **level (fixed) premium**, and builds **cash value** (a living benefit you can borrow against). Think of it as **buying** a house: once it's paid for, you still own it. More expensive than term because of the living benefits.

The five "types" of whole life differ **only in how the policy is paid** — all of them last to death/age 100, have a fixed premium, and accumulate cash value:
1. **Straight (ordinary) whole life** — premiums paid the insured's **entire life** (to death or age 100).
2. **Limited-pay whole life** — full lifetime coverage but premiums paid for a **limited time** (e.g., 20-pay life, life paid-up at 65). Shorter pay period → **higher premium** and **faster cash value** growth.
3. **Single-premium whole life** — entire premium paid in **one lump sum**; creates an **immediate cash value / nonforfeiture value**.
4. **Modified whole life** — **low** premiums in early years, then a **one-time** jump to a higher level premium that stays fixed.
5. **Graded premium whole life** — premium **increases yearly** for a stated number of years, then **levels off**.

## Part 4: Special-use (combination) policies

- **Family plan** — head of household on **whole life**; spouse and children added as **level term riders**, convertible to permanent **without evidence of insurability**. Children's premium is the **same regardless of the number of children**.
- **Family income policy** — whole life **+ decreasing term** measured from **date of purchase**. Pays monthly income to the beneficiary if death occurs during the period; after the period, only the face amount is paid (the decreasing term has expired). Uses **decreasing** term because income need shrinks as the household shrinks.
- **Family maintenance policy** — whole life **+ level term** measured from **date of death**. Pays income for a selected period **after** death, then **also** pays the full face amount. Level term "maintains" the family.
- **Multiple protection policy** — pays **double or triple** the face if death occurs during a specified period (e.g., to age 65); after the period, only the face is paid. Permanent + level term combination.
- **Joint life (first-to-die)** — one policy covers **two or more** lives; ages are **averaged**, single premium; pays the death benefit when the **first** insured dies; survivors may buy individual coverage without evidence of insurability. Premium is **less** than separate policies.
- **Joint and survivor / survivorship ("second-to-die")** — covers two lives, benefit paid on the death of the **last** surviving insured. Premium is **lower** than the combined premium for two separate policies. Common in estate planning.
- **Juvenile insurance** — written on the life of a **minor**; an adult is usually the payor. A **payor provision/rider** waives premiums until the child reaches a set age (18/21/25) if the **payor** dies or becomes disabled.
- **Credit life** — covers a **debtor**, pays off the loan balance if the debtor dies first; issued for **no more than the outstanding loan balance**; usually a **decreasing term** policy matched to the loan.

## Part 5: Nontraditional / interest-sensitive policies

- **Interest-sensitive (current assumption) whole life** — whole life whose cash value can grow **beyond the guarantee** if conditions warrant; premiums can vary with the insurer's death/investment/expense assumptions.
- **Adjustable life** — combines term and whole life in one plan; the owner can adjust **face amount and premium** as needs change without a new policy (evidence of insurability may be required to raise the death benefit).
- **Universal life (UL)** — flexible-premium, **flexible benefit** permanent insurance. The owner chooses the **amount and frequency** of premiums, which adjusts the face amount. Cash value earns at least a **minimum guaranteed interest rate**; any **surrender charges must be disclosed**. Two death-benefit options: level (Option A) and increasing (Option B).
- **Equity-indexed universal life (EIUL)** — UL whose cash value is tied to a **stock index** (e.g., S&P 500) with a **minimum guaranteed fixed rate** floor. Fixed-UL security with indexed growth potential.

## Part 6: Variable products — securities

Because investment risk transfers from the **insurer to the policyowner**, variable products are **securities** as well as insurance contracts. The producer must hold a securities registration (**FINRA**, historically NASD) **and** a life license to sell them; cash value sits in the insurer's **separate account**.

- **Variable whole life** — **fixed premiums**, a **guaranteed minimum death benefit** that can fluctuate above the minimum, and **cash values that fluctuate and are not guaranteed** (invested in separate accounts).
- **Variable universal life (VUL)** — combines variable (separate-account investing, owner picks the subaccounts) with universal (**flexible premiums, adjustable death benefit**). Has a guaranteed minimum death benefit and growth potential with risk of loss. Evidence of insurability can be required when the death benefit is **increased**.

## Part 7: Modified Endowment Contract (MEC) — the overfunding trap

A life policy that is **overfunded** under IRS limits becomes a **Modified Endowment Contract**. A policy that **fails the 7-pay test** (pays in more during the first **7 years** than the limit) is an MEC and **loses favorable tax treatment**.

- Withdrawals are taxed **LIFO** (earnings come out first, fully taxable as ordinary income).
- Distributions before age **59½** carry a **10% penalty** (penalty can apply to policy **loans** from a MEC).
- Taxation only occurs **when cash is distributed**. Once a MEC, always a MEC.

## Drill card (the testable facts)
- Industrial = small burial face, weekly debit. Group = master contract, no evidence of insurability.
- Term = cheapest, no cash value, death benefit only during the term ("renting").
- Level term = lowest premium for a set period; Decreasing term = mortgage/debt (level premium, falling face); Increasing term = rising face; ART = renews yearly.
- Renewable = continue without proving insurability; Convertible = change to permanent without evidence of insurability. **Both reprice at attained age.**
- Whole life = permanent, matures at **100**, level premium, builds cash value ("buying").
- 5 whole-life types differ only in **how paid**: straight, limited-pay (higher premium/faster CV), single-premium (immediate CV), modified (one jump), graded (rises then levels).
- Family plan = whole life head + term riders on spouse/kids (same premium regardless of number of children).
- Family **income** = decreasing term, from date of **purchase**. Family **maintenance** = level term, from date of **death**, then pays face too.
- Joint life = first-to-die, averaged age. Survivorship = second-to-die, estate planning, lower premium.
- Juvenile = payor provision waives premium if **payor** dies/disabled.
- Credit life = decreasing term, capped at loan balance.
- UL = flexible premium + flexible benefit, min guaranteed interest, surrender charges disclosed.
- Variable = securities (separate account, FINRA + life license); cash value not guaranteed.
- MEC = fails **7-pay test**, taxed **LIFO**, **10%** penalty before **59½**.

## Exam traps
- *Renewal/conversion raises the premium because of **attained age**, even though insurability is guaranteed.* This is the single most-tested term concept.
- *Decreasing term (family income, credit, mortgage) vs. level term (family maintenance).* Match the falling-need product to the falling debt.
- *Variable products require a **securities** registration on top of the life license* — a license-only producer cannot sell them.
- *MEC status is triggered by the 7-pay test and is permanent;* it changes the **taxation**, not the death benefit.
- *Single-premium and limited-pay both build cash value faster, but only single-premium creates an **immediate** cash/nonforfeiture value.*
