# Massachusetts — Accident & Health Producer Exam (Series 16-52)
## Module 10.0: Federal Tax Considerations for Health Insurance
**Exam weight: 4% (≈4 of 100 questions) | Source: Internal Revenue Code / IRS publications (public domain) | Verified against Prometric MA Licensing Information Handbook, outline live date Aug 25, 2018 (current edition)**

> **How to study this module:** Four questions, and they almost all reduce to one rule of thumb: **somebody gets the tax break, but not both ends.** If premiums went in with pre-tax/deducted dollars, benefits come out taxable. If premiums were paid with after-tax dollars, benefits come out tax-free. Learn the one big exception (BOE) and the thresholds, and this module is free points.

---

## Part 1: Personally-Owned Coverage (Outline 10.1)

- **Disability income:** premiums are **NOT deductible** (personal expense) → benefits are received **income-tax-free**. The cleanest example of the rule of thumb.
- **Medical expense insurance:** premiums count toward the **itemized medical expense deduction**, deductible only to the extent total unreimbursed medical expenses exceed **7.5% of AGI**. Benefits (reimbursements of actual expenses) are **not taxable**.
- **Long-term care (tax-qualified policies):** premiums are deductible as medical expenses **within age-based annual limits** (indexed each year); benefits are **tax-free** up to actual costs or the indexed per-diem cap for indemnity-style payouts.

## Part 2: Employer Group Coverage (Outline 10.2)

- **Medical and dental expense plans:** employer-paid premiums are **deductible to the employer** as a business expense and are **NOT taxable income to employees**; benefits are tax-free. The most tax-favored arrangement in the module.
- **Group disability income (STD/LTD):** the rule of thumb in action —
  - Employer pays the premium → benefits **taxable** to the employee
  - Employee pays with **after-tax** dollars → benefits **tax-free**
  - Shared premium → benefits taxable **in proportion** to the employer-paid share
  - **FICA:** employer-paid disability benefits are subject to Social Security/Medicare tax during the **first 6 calendar months** after the employee last worked; after that, FICA stops.
- **Group long-term care:** employer premiums deductible, not income to employees, qualified benefits tax-free — but LTC **cannot** be offered through a cafeteria plan/FSA.
- **AD&D:** treated like medical/health coverage — employer premiums deductible, benefits generally received tax-free.

## Part 3: Self-Employed Owners (Outline 10.3)

- **Sole proprietors, partners, and LLC members** (and >2% S-corp shareholders) may deduct **100% of health insurance premiums** for themselves, spouses, and dependents as an **above-the-line** deduction (no itemizing, no 7.5% floor) — limited to earned income from the business and unavailable in months they're eligible for an employer-subsidized plan.
- The 100% deduction covers **medical, dental, and tax-qualified LTC** (LTC within the age-based limits) — but **NOT disability income** premiums.

## Part 4: Business Disability Insurance (Outline 10.4)

| Product | Premiums deductible? | Benefits taxable? |
|---|---|---|
| **Key person DI** (business owns, pays, receives) | **No** | **No** — received tax-free |
| **Disability buy-sell** | **No** | **No** — proceeds fund the buyout tax-free |
| **Business overhead expense (BOE)** | **YES — the exception** | **Yes** — but they pay deductible overhead, washing out |

- *Exam trap:* BOE inverts the usual pattern — premiums deductible, benefits taxable. It works because the benefits reimburse expenses that are themselves deductible.

## Quick-Number Drill Card

| Item | Rule |
|---|---|
| Personal DI | Premiums not deductible → benefits tax-FREE |
| Employer-paid group DI | Premiums deductible to employer → benefits TAXABLE to employee |
| Shared-premium DI | Benefits taxable in proportion to employer share |
| 6 months | FICA applies to employer-paid disability benefits after last day worked |
| 7.5% of AGI | Itemized medical-expense deduction floor (personal) |
| 100% above the line | Self-employed health insurance deduction |
| Age-based limits (indexed) | Deductible tax-qualified LTC premium caps |
| Key person / buy-sell | Premiums NOT deductible, benefits NOT taxable |
| BOE | The inversion: premiums deductible, benefits taxable |

## Practice Questions

**1.** Renee personally owns a disability income policy and pays the premiums herself. When she becomes disabled, her monthly benefits are:
A) Fully taxable  B) Taxable above 7.5% of AGI  C) Received free of federal income tax  D) Taxable only after 6 months
> **C.** Nondeductible personal premiums buy tax-free benefits. (Outline 10.1)

**2.** An employer pays 100% of the group LTD premium. An employee's $3,000 monthly LTD benefit is:
A) Tax-free  B) Fully taxable as income to the employee  C) Taxable only to the employer  D) Tax-free if disability exceeds one year
> **B.** Employer-paid premiums (deducted by the employer, excluded from the employee's income) make the benefits taxable income to the employee. (Outline 10.2)

**3.** A partnership splits LTD premiums 50/50 with employees, who pay their half after-tax. Benefits received are:
A) Fully taxable  B) Fully tax-free  C) 50% taxable — proportional to the employer-paid share  D) Taxable only above 7.5% of AGI
> **C.** Shared premiums produce proportionally taxable benefits. (Outline 10.2)

**4.** Which business disability product features tax-DEDUCTIBLE premiums?
A) Key person disability income  B) Disability buy-sell  C) Business overhead expense  D) All business DI premiums are deductible
> **C.** BOE is the exception: premiums deductible, benefits taxable (and spent on deductible overhead). Key person and buy-sell premiums are nondeductible, benefits tax-free. (Outline 10.4)

**5.** A sole proprietor's premiums for her own family's medical coverage are:
A) Deductible only above 7.5% of AGI  B) 100% deductible above the line, up to her business earned income  C) Not deductible  D) Deductible only through a cafeteria plan
> **B.** The self-employed health insurance deduction takes 100% above the line — no itemizing required. (Outline 10.3)
