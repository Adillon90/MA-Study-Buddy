# Massachusetts — Accident & Health Producer Exam (Series 16-52)
## Module 7.0: Large Group Health Insurance
**Exam weight: 15% (≈15 of 100 questions) | Source law: M.G.L. c. 175 §§ 108C, 110, 110D, 110G, 110I; c. 176J; 211 CMR 38.00, 66.00; federal COBRA/Civil Rights Act | Verified against Prometric MA Licensing Information Handbook, outline live date Aug 25, 2018 (current edition)**

> **How to study this module:** The group-insurance mechanics (master contract, certificates, rating, COB) are universal. The exam money is in the continuation-of-coverage cluster: federal COBRA layered on top of THREE separate Massachusetts continuation statutes, each with its own trigger and its own clock — 31 days, 39 weeks, and "until remarriage." Candidates who keep those four straight pick up easy points; candidates who blur them lose them.

---

## Part 1: Characteristics of Group Insurance (Outline 7.1)

- **One master contract** is issued to the group sponsor (the employer/policyholder). Covered employees don't receive policies — they receive **certificates of coverage** (also called certificates of insurance) summarizing their benefits. Employees are **certificate holders**, not policyowners.
- **No individual underwriting** — the group is underwritten as a unit, which is why group coverage is generally cheaper and available regardless of an individual's health.
- **Experience rating vs. community rating:** large groups are typically **experience rated** — premiums reflect the group's own claims history. **Community rating** prices everyone in a broad pool the same way; in Massachusetts, the merged individual/small-group market under c. 176J uses modified community rating with only limited adjustment factors.

## Part 2: Eligible Groups and Marketing (Outlines 7.2–7.3)

- Groups must exist **for a purpose other than buying insurance** — primarily **employment-related groups** (individual employer groups in this exam's scope).
- **Regulatory jurisdiction:** a group plan is governed by the law of the state where the **master policy is delivered** — which is why the outline stresses "place of delivery." Massachusetts extends several of its mandates to out-of-state group policies that cover MA residents (the continuation statutes below say so explicitly).
- Group advertising is subject to the same unfair-practice rules as individual marketing (Module 1).

## Part 3: Employer Group Underwriting and Eligibility (Outline 7.4)

### What the insurer underwrites (since it can't underwrite individuals)
The **group's characteristics**: size, industry, age/sex mix, turnover (persistency), plan design (cost-sharing levels), prior claims experience, and the employer's **administrative capability** to handle enrollment and premium collection. MA nondiscrimination statutes (175:108C and parallels) bar discriminatory group practices.

### Employee and dependent eligibility — 175:110; 211 CMR 66.04
Typical eligibility requires being a **full-time, actively-at-work employee**; new hires may face a probationary period, then enroll during their eligibility window or wait for the **annual open enrollment**. Dependent eligibility follows the rules from Module 6 — children to age 26, newborns from the moment of birth, adopted children from petition/placement.

### Coordination of Benefits (COB) — 211 CMR 38.01–.08
When a person is covered by two group plans, COB rules decide payment order so combined benefits never exceed 100% of the expense:
- A plan covering the person **as an employee pays primary**; the plan covering them as a dependent pays secondary.
- For dependent children of married parents, the **birthday rule** applies: the plan of the parent whose **birthday falls earlier in the calendar year** is primary (not the older parent — the earlier birthday).
- For divorced/separated parents, a court decree controls; otherwise the custodial parent's plan generally pays first.

### Changing insurance companies — no-loss/no-gain
When an employer switches carriers, employees should neither lose nor gain by the changeover: the new plan picks up ongoing claims without imposing fresh probationary periods (**no-loss no-gain**), and **deductible and coinsurance credits carry over** for amounts already satisfied that year.

### Extension of benefits
An employee who is **totally disabled** when the group coverage terminates continues to receive benefits **for that disabling condition** for a limited extension period — the disability doesn't lose coverage just because the plan ended.

## Part 4: The Continuation Cluster — Federal COBRA + Three MA Statutes

### Federal COBRA (employers with 20+ employees)
Qualified beneficiaries may continue the group coverage at their own cost after a qualifying event:
- **18 months** — employee's termination (other than gross misconduct) or reduction in hours
- **29 months** — the 18-month event where the beneficiary is disabled (Social Security definition) during the first 60 days of continuation
- **36 months** — employee's death, divorce/legal separation, employee's Medicare entitlement, or a child aging out of dependent status
- **Election period: 60 days** from the later of the qualifying event or the COBRA notice
- Premium: up to **102%** of the group rate (up to 150% during the disability-extension months)

### Massachusetts continuation statutes — keep the triggers straight

| Statute | Trigger | What continues, and for how long |
|---|---|---|
| **175:110D** | Insured **leaves the group** (any reason) | Coverage continues automatically for **31 days** after leaving, unless entitled to similar benefits elsewhere |
| **175:110G** | **Involuntary layoff** or **death** of the member | Coverage for the member (or surviving spouse) and dependents continues **39 weeks** from ineligibility — or until they become eligible under another group plan, whichever is first; never longer than their most recent period of coverage. The employer must notify them of the right. |
| **175:110I** | **Divorce or separate support judgment** | The former spouse **remains eligible under the member's plan without additional premium or examination**, as if the judgment had not entered (unless the judgment says otherwise) — until the **remarriage of either** the member or the former spouse, or as the judgment provides. If the *member* remarries, the judgment may give the former spouse the right to continue via a rider or an individual plan at fair additional premium. |

- These MA statutes apply to group policies issued **within or without the commonwealth** that cover Massachusetts residents.
- **Small group continuation — c. 176J § 9 ("Mini-COBRA," verified):** Massachusetts extends COBRA-parallel continuation rights to small-group plans below the federal 20-employee threshold. Key verified mechanics: coverage must be **identical** to what similarly situated active members receive; it may not be conditioned on **evidence of insurability**; the disability extension follows the federal pattern (notice to the carrier within **60 days** of the disability determination and before the 18-month period ends), with the disabled individual chargeable **150%** of premium after month 18; and when continuation expires, the carrier must offer enrollment in a **conversion nongroup plan** during the **180-day period** ending on the expiration date. One trap that's pure Massachusetts: Mini-COBRA does **NOT** apply to dental or vision plans.
- *Exam traps:* 110G's clock runs in **weeks (39)**, not months. 110I has **no fixed clock** — it runs to remarriage or the judgment's terms, and costs the former spouse nothing extra while it lasts. 110D's 31 days happens automatically with no election.

## Part 5: Small Employer and Individual Medical Plans (Outline 7.5)

- **Small employer (176J:1; 211 CMR 66.04, verified):** Massachusetts merged its individual and small-group markets into one risk pool; "eligible small business" runs from **1 to 50 eligible employees** (carriers may require groups of 5 or fewer to enroll through an intermediary or the Connector).
- **Guaranteed availability and modified community rating (211 CMR 66.07, verified):** carriers must offer plans to all eligible small groups, premiums are based on the **collective experience of the merged pool**, and a carrier may apply **only the enumerated rating adjustment factors** — notably an **age factor capped at a 2:1 ratio** (highest adult factor to lowest, ACA-aligned) and an **area factor between 0.8 and 1.2** — with all other rating adjustments **prohibited**. Health status and claims experience can never be rating factors. Annual rate filings with actuarial certification are reviewed by the Division (66.08).
- **Guaranteed renewability** (211 CMR 66.06): small-group plans renew at the employer's option except for statutory causes (nonpayment, fraud, market withdrawal).

## Part 6: Federal Regulation of Employer Plans (Outline 7.6)

- **Civil Rights Act / Pregnancy Discrimination Act:** employers with **15+ employees** must treat pregnancy, childbirth, and related conditions the same as any other medical condition in their health plans — same benefits, same cost-sharing.
- **Relationship with Medicare:** for active employees 65+ in groups of **20+ employees**, the **group plan pays primary and Medicare is secondary** (Medicare Secondary Payer rules — same rule from Module 9). Employers may offer Medicare **carve-outs or supplements** for retirees, where Medicare pays primary and the employer plan fills gaps.

## Practice Questions

**1.** In group health insurance, the employees covered under the plan receive:
A) Individual policies  B) Certificates of coverage under the employer's master contract  C) Riders to the master policy  D) Memorandum copies of the master policy
> **B.** One master contract is issued to the policyholder (the employer); covered employees are certificate holders. (Outline 7.1)

**2.** Tom and Maria both cover their daughter under their respective employers' plans. Tom's birthday is March 3, 1985; Maria's is January 20, 1988. Which plan pays the daughter's claims first?
A) Tom's, because he is older  B) Maria's, because her birthday falls earlier in the calendar year  C) Whichever plan has higher benefits  D) Both split equally
> **B.** The COB birthday rule looks at which parent's birthday falls earlier in the calendar year — not which parent is older. January 20 beats March 3. (211 CMR 38)

**3.** Carla is laid off when her Massachusetts employer downsizes. Under M.G.L. c. 175 § 110G, her group health coverage continues for up to:
A) 18 months  B) 31 days  C) 39 weeks from the date of ineligibility  D) 60 days
> **C.** Involuntary layoff (or the member's death) triggers continuation for 39 weeks — ending earlier if she becomes eligible under another group plan, and never exceeding her most recent period of coverage. (175:110G)

**4.** After Dave and Ellen divorce, the judgment is silent about health insurance. Ellen was covered as Dave's spouse under his employer's plan. Under M.G.L. c. 175 § 110I, Ellen:
A) Loses coverage at the judgment date  B) Remains eligible under Dave's plan without additional premium, until either of them remarries  C) Must elect COBRA within 60 days  D) Converts to an individual policy at her own cost
> **B.** The former spouse remains eligible as if the judgment had not entered — no extra premium, no examination — until the remarriage of either party or as the judgment provides. (175:110I)

**5.** Under federal COBRA, an employee terminated for ordinary performance reasons may continue group coverage for ___, paying up to ___ of the group premium.
A) 36 months; 100%  B) 18 months; 102%  C) 29 months; 110%  D) 12 months; 102%
> **B.** Termination (other than gross misconduct) and reduced hours are 18-month events; the premium may be up to 102% of the group rate. (COBRA)

**6.** A qualifying event entitling a beneficiary to 36 months of COBRA continuation is:
A) The employee's reduction in hours  B) The employee's termination  C) A dependent child aging out of the plan  D) The employer changing carriers
> **C.** The 36-month events are death, divorce/legal separation, the employee's Medicare entitlement, and a child losing dependent status. Termination and reduced hours are 18-month events; a carrier change is not a qualifying event at all. (COBRA)

**7.** When a Massachusetts employer switches group carriers mid-year, the "no-loss no-gain" principle means:
A) Premiums cannot change  B) Ongoing claims transfer to the new carrier without new probationary exclusions, and deductible credits carry over  C) Employees must re-enroll and re-satisfy deductibles  D) The old carrier remains liable for all future claims
> **B.** Employees should be no worse off for the changeover: continuing claims are honored and amounts already credited toward deductibles/coinsurance carry forward. (Outline 7.4)

**8.** A Massachusetts carrier may vary small-group premiums based on all of the following EXCEPT:
A) Geographic area  B) Age band  C) The group's claims experience  D) Group size
> **C.** The merged-market rating rules permit only limited factors; health status and claims experience are prohibited rating factors for small groups. (c. 176J; 211 CMR 66.08)
