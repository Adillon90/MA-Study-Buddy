# Massachusetts — Accident & Health Producer Exam (Series 16-52)
## Module 8.0: Dental Insurance
**Exam weight: 2% (≈2 of 100 questions) | Source: universal dental plan doctrine per the Series 16-52 outline | Verified against Prometric MA Licensing Information Handbook, outline live date Aug 25, 2018 (current edition)**

> **How to study this module:** Two questions on the exam, and they come from a short list: the three benefit tiers and their typical cost-sharing, predetermination of benefits, and how group dental fights adverse selection. Fifteen minutes of study buys two points.

---

## Part 1: Types of Dental Treatment (Outline 8.1)

- **Diagnostic and preventive** — exams, X-rays, cleanings, fluoride. The category plans want people to use: typically covered at or near **100%, often with no deductible**, because prevention is cheaper than repair.
- **Restorative** — repairing teeth: fillings, crowns, and the related specialty categories (endodontics/root canals, periodontics/gum treatment, prosthodontics/bridges and dentures, oral surgery, orthodontics).

## Part 2: Indemnity Dental Plans (Outline 8.2)

- **Choice of providers:** traditional indemnity dental lets the patient see any dentist.
- **Scheduled vs. nonscheduled:** scheduled plans pay **fixed dollar amounts per procedure** (a fee schedule); nonscheduled (comprehensive) plans pay on a **usual/reasonable/customary** basis with coinsurance.
- **The three benefit tiers and the classic cost-sharing ladder:**
  1. **Diagnostic/preventive services** — ~100%, deductible usually waived
  2. **Basic services** (fillings, extractions, root canals) — commonly ~80% after deductible
  3. **Major services** (crowns, bridges, dentures) — commonly ~50% after deductible
- **Deductibles and coinsurance** apply mostly to basic and major tiers; plans carry **annual benefit maximums** (a dollar cap per person per year) — dental's signature feature, the opposite of medical plans' out-of-pocket caps.
- **Combination plans** mix the approaches — scheduled amounts for some tiers, nonscheduled for others.
- **Exclusions and limitations:** cosmetic work, duplicate appliances, replacement frequency limits (one crown per tooth per X years), and missing-tooth limitations.
- **Predetermination of benefits:** for expensive proposed work, the dentist submits the treatment plan to the insurer **before treatment**, and the insurer specifies what it will pay. Not a guarantee of outcome — a pre-treatment estimate that prevents surprise bills. (The dental cousin of prospective utilization review.)

## Part 3: Employer Group Dental (Outline 8.3)

- **Integrated vs. stand-alone deductibles:** an *integrated* plan folds dental into the major-medical deductible (one combined deductible); a *stand-alone* plan has its own, usually small, dental deductible.
- **Minimizing adverse selection** — the central group-dental underwriting problem, because people can schedule dental work to coincide with gaining coverage. Plan defenses:
  - **Waiting/probationary periods** before major services are covered
  - Limiting enrollment to initial eligibility and **open enrollment windows** (and penalizing late entrants with reduced benefits or longer waits)
  - **Annual maximums** and tiered coinsurance that keep the plan from absorbing stockpiled treatment all at once

## Practice Questions

**1.** Under a typical nonscheduled dental plan, a crown (major service) is most likely covered at:
A) 100% with no deductible  B) 80% after deductible  C) 50% after deductible  D) A flat $50 schedule amount
> **C.** The classic ladder: preventive ~100%, basic ~80%, major ~50% after the deductible — and subject to the annual maximum. (Outline 8.2)

**2.** Before beginning a $6,000 reconstruction, a dentist submits the treatment plan to the insurer, which responds with the amount it will pay. This process is:
A) Concurrent review  B) Predetermination of benefits  C) Subrogation  D) A benefit schedule
> **B.** Predetermination gives a pre-treatment estimate of plan payment for proposed expensive work. (Outline 8.2)

**3.** Group dental plans impose waiting periods for major services and penalize late enrollees primarily to:
A) Comply with ERISA  B) Minimize adverse selection by people who enroll only when they need work done  C) Reduce paperwork  D) Coordinate with Medicare
> **B.** Dental work is postponable, making adverse selection dental underwriting's central problem; waiting periods and enrollment limits are the standard defenses. (Outline 8.3)

**4.** A dental plan whose deductible is combined with the employee's major medical deductible is called:
A) Stand-alone  B) Scheduled  C) Integrated  D) Combination
> **C.** Integrated plans share one deductible with the medical plan; stand-alone dental carries its own. (Outline 8.3)
