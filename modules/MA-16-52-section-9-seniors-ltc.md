# Massachusetts — Accident & Health Producer Exam (Series 16-52)
## Module 9.0: Insurance for Senior Citizens and Special Needs Individuals
**Exam weight: 15% (≈15 of 100 questions) | Source law: Federal Medicare statute/CMS; M.G.L. c. 176K; 211 CMR 71.00; 211 CMR 65.00; c. 118E | Verified against Prometric MA Licensing Information Handbook, outline live date Aug 25, 2018 (current edition); MA Medigap plan structure verified against the Division's 2026 carrier chart**

> **How to study this module:** Three blocks. Medicare itself is pure federal content — memorize the Part A/B structure and the enrollment windows. Medicare supplements are where Massachusetts breaks from the rest of the country: MA is one of only three waiver states with its own Medigap designs (**Core, Supplement 1, Supplement 1A**) instead of the national lettered A–N plans — national prep books routinely teach this wrong for MA. Long-term care runs on 211 CMR 65 plus the federal tax-qualified triggers. Note: Medicare dollar amounts (deductibles, coinsurance) reset every year, so this module teaches the *structure* — the exam tests structure, not this year's dollar figures.

## Part 1: Medicare (Outline 9.1)

Federal health insurance for people **65 and older**, plus younger people receiving **Social Security disability benefits (after 24 months)** and people with **end-stage renal disease (ESRD)** or ALS. Administered by **CMS**; funded by payroll taxes (Part A), premiums, and general revenue.

### Part A — Hospital Insurance
- **Premium-free** for those with **40 quarters (10 years)** of Medicare-covered employment (or a spouse with them); others may buy in.
- Organized around **benefit periods**: begins on inpatient admission, ends when the patient has been out of the hospital/SNF for **60 consecutive days**. The Part A deductible applies **per benefit period** — not per year. A patient can pay it more than once in a year.
- Inpatient hospital: after the deductible, days **1–60** fully covered; days **61–90** require a daily coinsurance; beyond 90, the patient draws on **60 lifetime reserve days** (one lifetime pool, never renewed), each with a higher daily coinsurance.
- **Skilled nursing facility:** requires a prior **3-day inpatient hospital stay**. Days **1–20** fully covered; days **21–100** daily coinsurance; **nothing after day 100** per benefit period. Custodial-only care is NOT covered.
- Also covers: **hospice** (terminally ill, electing comfort care), **home health care**, and blood **after the first 3 pints**.

### Part B — Medical Insurance
- **Optional**, funded by a **monthly premium** (income-adjusted) plus general revenue.
- After an **annual deductible**, Medicare pays **80%** of the approved amount; the patient owes **20%** — with **no out-of-pocket cap** under Original Medicare. That missing cap is the reason Medigap exists.
- Covers: physician services, outpatient care, durable medical equipment, lab work, preventive services.
- Key exclusions: most dental, vision, hearing aids, and — critically — **long-term custodial care**.

### Enrollment windows
- **Initial Enrollment Period:** 7 months — the 3 months before the 65th-birthday month, the birthday month, and the 3 months after.
- **General Enrollment Period:** January 1 – March 31 each year, for those who missed the IEP.
- **Special Enrollment Period:** for those who kept working with employer group coverage — 8 months after that employment/coverage ends, penalty-free.
- **Late penalties:** Part B premium rises **10% for each full 12-month period** of delayed enrollment (lifetime); Part D carries roughly **1% per month** delayed.

### Part C — Medicare Advantage
Private plans (mostly HMO/PPO) that replace Original Medicare's payment structure: must cover everything A and B cover (hospice stays with Original Medicare), usually bundle Part D, often add dental/vision, and typically use provider networks with an annual out-of-pocket maximum. The enrollee still pays the Part B premium.

### Part D — Prescription Drug Coverage
Private, optional drug plans (standalone PDP or inside Medicare Advantage). Formularies and pharmacy networks apply; a federal **annual out-of-pocket cap on covered drugs ($2,000, indexed)** applies as of 2025. Late enrollment penalty applies without creditable drug coverage.

## Part 2: Medicare Supplements — Where Massachusetts Is Different (Outline 9.2)

### The national system MA does NOT use
In 47 states, Medigap policies are standardized into lettered plans (A–N). **Massachusetts, Minnesota, and Wisconsin hold federal waivers** and design their own plans. An MA candidate who studies the lettered plans from a national prep book is studying the wrong system.

### The Massachusetts plans — c. 176K; 211 CMR 71.08 and Appendices
- **Core Plan** — the basic package: Part A hospital coinsurance (plus extension days after Medicare benefits end), Part B 20% coinsurance, hospice cost-sharing, first 3 pints of blood, and MA-mandated benefits including **60 days per calendar year of inpatient mental health** care.
- **Supplement 1** — Core plus: **Part A deductible, skilled nursing facility coinsurance, Part B deductible, foreign travel emergency**, and expanded inpatient mental health (**120 days per benefit period**). Roughly comparable to the old national Plan F.
- **Supplement 1A** (added 2020) — identical to Supplement 1 **except it does not cover the Part B deductible**.
- **Why 1A exists:** federal law (**MACRA**) bars anyone **newly eligible for Medicare on or after January 1, 2020** from buying a Medigap plan that covers the Part B deductible. So: eligible before 2020 → may buy Core, Supplement 1, or 1A; eligible 2020 or later → Core or 1A only. **This is a prime exam-trap fact.**
- MA also bars providers from charging **Part B excess charges**, so MA plans don't need (or offer) excess-charge coverage.

### Enrollment and consumer protections — 176K; 211 CMR 71.10 (verified)
- **Guaranteed issue, always:** no issuer may **at any time** deny, condition, or price a MA Medigap policy based on **age, health status, claims experience, receipt of health care, medical condition, or genetic information** (71.10(1)) — MA Medigap is effectively **community rated**, and genetic tests can never be required.
- **No pre-existing condition limitations or waiting periods — ever** (71.10(2)). MA Medigap policies may not contain them at all. (National materials describing 6-month pre-ex waits do not apply in Massachusetts.)
- **Required Part B open enrollment** (71.10(3)): the federal-style **6-month window** beginning the first month the person is 65+ and enrolled in Part B is codified as a floor.
- **Continuous open enrollment** (71.10(9)): issuers may hold year-round open enrollment (filed with the Commissioner 30 days before starting; 60 days' notice to stop) — and per the Division's official carrier chart, MA carriers currently all do, which is why applicants can enroll or switch any time. Surcharges are permitted only for **late enrollees** without continuous prior creditable coverage and for coverage upgrades (71.20).
- Marketing protections under 211 CMR 71: standards for marketing and advertising, **appropriateness of recommended purchase** (no excessive insurance), required disclosure and **buyer's guide** delivery, **right to return** (free look), replacement rules, reporting of multiple policies, and permitted compensation limits.

## Part 3: Other Options for Individuals with Medicare (Outline 9.3)

- **Working past 65 with employer group coverage (20+ employees):** the **group plan pays primary, Medicare secondary**. The employee may delay Part B penalty-free (SEP applies later).
- **Disabled employees and ESRD:** employer plans coordinate with Medicare under the secondary-payer rules (ESRD: group plan primary for the initial coordination period).
- **MassHealth (c. 118E)** — Massachusetts Medicaid. Income/asset-based eligibility; for "dual eligibles" it pays Medicare premiums and cost-sharing, and it is the payer of last resort for **long-term custodial care** that Medicare excludes — after the person has spent down assets.

## Part 4: Long-Term Care Insurance — 211 CMR 65.00 (Outline 9.4)

### What it covers
The care Medicare doesn't: extended **custodial, intermediate, and skilled care**, delivered in nursing facilities or alternatives — **home health care** (211 CMR 65.05(2)(c)), **assisted living, adult day care,** and **respite care** (temporary relief for a family caregiver — a favorite exam definition).

### Benefit structure
- **Benefit period** (years of coverage or a dollar pool), **daily/monthly benefit amount**, and an **elimination period** (waiting days before benefits start) set the price.
- **Benefit triggers** (211 CMR 65.05(1)) align with the federal tax-qualified standard: inability to perform **2 of 6 Activities of Daily Living** — bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, continence, eating — expected to last **at least 90 days**, OR **severe cognitive impairment**, certified by a licensed health care practitioner.
- Optional benefits: **guarantee of insurability** (buy more coverage later without underwriting) and **return of premium**.

### Massachusetts required provisions — 211 CMR 65 (verified)
- **Inflation adjustment benefit must be offered** (65.06(1)) — the buyer can decline, but the insurer must offer it.
- **Nonforfeiture benefit must be offered** (65.06(2)) — some value preserved if the policy lapses after years of premiums.
- **"Your Options for Financing Long-Term Care"** consumer guide must be delivered (65.09), plus an **outline of coverage** (65.101) and **policy illustration** (65.100).
- **Suitability of recommended purchase** — producers must assess whether LTC insurance fits the buyer's finances, and agent training/marketing standards apply (65.08).
- **Unintentional lapse protection (65.10, verified):** no policy may be **issued** until the applicant either designates **at least one person besides themselves** to receive lapse notices or signs a dated **written waiver** declining to. No policy may lapse for nonpayment unless the carrier notifies the insured **and the designee at least 30 days before** the lapse takes effect, by first-class mail (deemed given 10 days after mailing), and the notice can't even be sent until the premium is 30 days overdue. Every policy must also include **reinstatement** if the policyholder proves they were **cognitively impaired or had lost functional capacity** before the grace period expired — the protection for exactly the people LTC insurance serves.
- **Minimum standards (65.05, verified):** elimination periods may not exceed **365 days**; benefits may NOT be conditioned on **prior hospitalization**, Medicare ineligibility, "steady improvement," or (with narrow exceptions) medical-necessity standards. Post-claims underwriting is prohibited (65.11).
- **Right to return (free look) — verified at c. 176U § 3(e):** the applicant may return an LTC policy within **30 days of delivery** for any reason, with the premium refunded **within 30 days** of the return — and the right must be **prominently printed on or attached to the first page**.
- **MassHealth exemption (c. 118E § 33; 130 CMR 515.014, verified):** a permanently institutionalized person's **home is protected from MassHealth liens/recovery** if, when purchased, their LTC policy met 211 CMR 65 standards — in practice: at least **730 days (2 years)** of nursing-facility benefits, at least **$125 per day**, and an elimination period of **no more than 365 days**. This is the state's concrete incentive to buy private LTC coverage.

## Practice Questions

**1.** Gloria is hospitalized for 10 days, goes home, and is readmitted 70 days later. Her Part A deductible:
A) Is waived for the second stay  B) Applies again — a new benefit period began  C) Applies once per calendar year  D) Is prorated
> **B.** A benefit period ends after 60 consecutive days out of the hospital/SNF; her readmission on day 70 starts a new benefit period and a new deductible. (CMS, Part A)

**2.** Frank wants Medicare to cover his skilled nursing facility stay. Coverage requires:
A) Any prior doctor visit  B) A prior inpatient hospital stay of at least 3 days  C) Exhaustion of his lifetime reserve days  D) A Medigap policy
> **B.** SNF coverage requires a qualifying 3-day inpatient hospital stay; days 1–20 are then fully covered and 21–100 carry daily coinsurance. (CMS, Part A)

**3.** Doris turned 65 and enrolled in Part B in March 2026. Which Medicare supplement plans may she buy in Massachusetts?
A) Any lettered plan A through N  B) Core, Supplement 1, or Supplement 1A  C) Core or Supplement 1A only  D) Supplement 1 only
> **C.** MA sells only its waiver-state plans, and because she became eligible after January 1, 2020, MACRA bars her from any plan covering the Part B deductible — which rules out Supplement 1. (c. 176K; 211 CMR 71.08; MACRA)

**4.** The ONLY benefit difference between MA Supplement 1 and Supplement 1A is:
A) Foreign travel emergency  B) The Part A deductible  C) The Part B deductible  D) Skilled nursing coinsurance
> **C.** Supplement 1A is Supplement 1 without Part B deductible coverage. (211 CMR 71.08)

**5.** Henry, 67, still works for a 500-employee company and has its group health plan. Which pays his hospital claims first?
A) Medicare; the group plan is secondary  B) The employer group plan; Medicare is secondary  C) They split 50/50  D) Whichever he files with first
> **B.** With an employer of 20+ employees, the group plan pays primary for a working 65+ employee under the Medicare secondary payer rules. (Outline 9.3)

**6.** Under a tax-qualified LTC policy, benefits are triggered when the insured cannot perform:
A) 1 of 6 ADLs for 30 days  B) 2 of 6 ADLs, expected to last at least 90 days, or has severe cognitive impairment  C) 3 of 6 ADLs for any duration  D) Any ADL, with a physician note
> **B.** The trigger is 2 of 6 ADLs (bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, continence, eating) expected to last 90+ days, or severe cognitive impairment, certified by a licensed practitioner. (211 CMR 65.05(1); HIPAA)

**7.** Massachusetts LTC regulations require insurers to OFFER (though the buyer may decline) all of the following EXCEPT:
A) An inflation adjustment benefit  B) A nonforfeiture benefit  C) Designation of a third party to receive lapse notices  D) A premium discount for MassHealth enrollees
> **D.** The required offers are inflation protection (65.06(1)), nonforfeiture (65.06(2)), and the third-party lapse designee (65.10). There is no MassHealth premium discount; the MassHealth interaction is the lien/recovery exemption. (211 CMR 65)

**8.** An adult daughter caring for her father at home needs a two-week break. The LTC benefit designed for this situation is:
A) Respite care  B) Hospice  C) Concurrent review  D) Custodial conversion
> **A.** Respite care provides temporary professional care to relieve a family caregiver. (211 CMR 65; outline 9.4)
