# Massachusetts — Accident & Health Producer Exam (Series 16-52)
## Module 3.0: Accident, Health, or Sickness Insurance Basics
**Exam weight: 11% (≈11 of 100 questions) | Source law: M.G.L. c. 175 §§ 108E, 108H–108I, 110E, 110(N), 146B; c. 111M; 211 CMR 36, 40, 42; 956 CMR 5.00 | Verified against Prometric MA Licensing Information Handbook, outline live date Aug 25, 2018 (current edition)**

> **How to study this module:** Half vocabulary, half producer conduct. The definitions and policy classes are universal. The Massachusetts layer is concentrated in producer responsibilities (marketing regs, the guaranty-association gag rule, replacement requirements) and two MA-distinctive rules: the underwriting limits on HIV and genetic information, and the **individual mandate** — Massachusetts has required residents to carry health coverage since 2006, and unlike the federal version, the MA penalty still has teeth.

## Part 1: Perils, Losses, and Policy Classes (Outlines 3.1–3.3)

### The two perils
- **Accidental injury** — bodily harm from an accident: sudden, unforeseen, unintended. Policies historically used either "accidental **means**" wording (the *cause* must be accidental — stricter) or the modern "accidental **bodily injury/results**" wording (the *result* must be accidental — broader, more favorable to the insured).
- **Sickness** — illness or disease first manifesting after the policy's effective date.

### The four principal loss/benefit types
1. **Loss of income from disability** — replaces wages (Module 5)
2. **Hospital/medical expense** — pays cost of care (Module 6)
3. **Dental/vision expense** (Module 8)
4. **Long-term care / home health expense** (Module 9)

### Classes of policies
- **Individual vs. group** — own policy and underwriting vs. certificate under a master contract
- **Private vs. government** — commercial/BCBS/HMO coverage vs. Medicare, Medicaid/MassHealth, workers' compensation, Social Security disability
- **Limited vs. comprehensive** — and the Massachusetts terminology twist: limited-benefit products are classified as **accident/sickness policies**, while comprehensive medical coverage is **health insurance**. The exam outline itself is organized around this distinction.

## Part 2: Limited Policies (Outline 3.4)

Limited policies pay only for **specifically defined perils or limited amounts**, and Massachusetts requires a prominent **notice to the insured** that the coverage is limited. Types to recognize:
- **Accidental Death & Dismemberment (AD&D)** — pays the **principal sum** for accidental death (or both hands/feet/sight), **capital sum** (a fraction, often 50%) for lesser dismemberments
- **Specified (dread) disease** — one named disease, e.g., cancer-only
- **Hospital indemnity** — a fixed dollar amount **per day hospitalized**, paid regardless of actual expenses
- **Credit disability** — pays the insured's loan payments during disability; the creditor is beneficiary
- **Blanket insurance** — covers a changing group (sports team members, bus passengers, students) without naming individuals
- Prescription drug, dental-only, vision-only, and Medicare supplement policies

## Part 3: Government Plans, MSAs, and HSAs (Outline 3.5)

- **Government plans** (Medicare, MassHealth, workers' comp) are excluded from private-policy duplication — covered elsewhere in the course.
- **Archer Medical Savings Accounts (MSAs):** the older vehicle — limited to **self-employed individuals and employees of small employers (50 or fewer)** paired with a high-deductible plan; contributions capped at a percentage of the deductible. Largely closed to new participants.
- **Health Savings Accounts (HSAs):** the current vehicle — requires enrollment in a qualified **high-deductible health plan (HDHP)**, no other disqualifying coverage, not enrolled in Medicare, not claimed as a dependent. The famous **triple tax advantage**: contributions deductible, growth tax-deferred, withdrawals for qualified medical expenses tax-free. Funds roll over year to year (no use-it-or-lose-it) and are portable.
- **Massachusetts child health program:** the Children's Medical Security Plan provides primary/preventive coverage for uninsured MA children — a state safety-net program, not private insurance.

## Part 4: Producer Responsibilities (Outline 3.6)

- **Marketing requirements (211 CMR 40.00) and advertising (175:110E):** advertisements must be truthful and not misleading; the Commissioner enforces form, content, and disclosure standards.
- **Guaranty Association gag rule (175:146B(19)):** the Life and Health Insurance Guaranty Association protects policyholders if an insurer becomes insolvent — but producers are **prohibited from using its existence as a sales tool** ("don't worry, the state guarantees it"). Classic exam question.
- **Outline of coverage (211 CMR 42.09):** must be delivered to the applicant at application (or with policy delivery if so structured) — a plain-language summary of benefits, exclusions, and renewability.
- **Disclosure of information about individuals (175:108E; 211 CMR 42.09)** and the privacy framework from c. 175I (Module 1).
- **Field underwriting:** the producer is the insurer's first line of risk selection — accurate, complete applications; reporting observed risk factors via the **producer report**; collecting premium; obtaining signatures.
- **Delivery requirements:** deliver the policy promptly; if no premium was collected with the application, obtain a **statement of continued good health** plus the premium for coverage to take effect.
- **Errors & omissions exposure:** the common producer failures — misstating coverage, failing to deliver, failing to forward premium, exceeding authority.

## Part 5: Individual Underwriting by the Insurer (Outline 3.7)

### Sources of underwriting information
- **The application** — the primary source; the applicant's statements are **representations** (not warranties)
- **Producer report** — the agent's observations; not seen by the applicant
- **Attending Physician Statement (APS)** — detail from the applicant's own doctor
- **Investigative consumer report** — interviews about character/lifestyle; FCRA notice rights apply (Module 1)
- **Medical Information Bureau (MIB)** — a nonprofit **member-insurer bureau** of coded prior-application data used to deter fraud and misrepresentation. The applicant must be notified and authorize; an insurer may **not decline solely** on MIB data.
- **Medical exams and lab tests, including HIV (211 CMR 36.05):** HIV testing requires the applicant's **written informed consent** on an approved form, with confidentiality protections; underwriting may not use **sexual orientation** in risk classification.
- **Genetic information (175:108H, 108I):** Massachusetts bars insurers from requiring **genetic tests** or using genetic information in accident & sickness underwriting without consent — a strong MA-specific protection.

### Risk classification
- **Preferred** — better than average; lowest rates
- **Standard** — average; normal rates
- **Substandard** — higher risk; issued with a rated premium, exclusion rider, or modified benefits — or declined

## Part 6: Replacement and the MA Individual Mandate (Outline 3.8)

### Replacing accident & sickness coverage — 211 CMR 42.08, 42.11 (verified)
Replacement deserves caution because the client can be hurt: new waiting periods (large-group pre-ex rules), benefits lost, exclusions gained, and underwriting at an older age. Verified Massachusetts mechanics: every **application form must contain a question** asking whether the new policy will replace existing accident and sickness coverage (42.08(1)). When the sale involves replacement, the agent or carrier must furnish the prescribed **"Notice to Applicant Regarding Replacement"** — **at the time of application or before the policy is issued** — leaving a copy with the applicant and keeping a **signed copy** in the carrier's records (42.08(2)). The prescribed notice (42.11) warns in substance that **existing health conditions may not be covered under the new policy** and urges comparison before terminating the old coverage. The producer's liability for a bad replacement is a textbook E&O scenario.

### The Massachusetts individual mandate — c. 111M; 956 CMR 5.00
Since 2006 (pre-dating the ACA, and surviving the federal penalty's repeal), Massachusetts adults **18+** must maintain **Minimum Creditable Coverage (MCC)** or face a **state tax penalty**, reported on **Schedule HC** with the MA income tax return. Penalties scale with income; exemptions exist for affordability hardship and religious belief. **Exam-critical nuance:** owning only a limited accident/sickness policy (hospital indemnity, dread disease) does **NOT** satisfy the mandate — MCC requires comprehensive coverage meeting state standards.

## Practice Questions

**1.** A policy paying $200 for each day the insured is hospitalized, regardless of actual charges, is a:
A) Major medical plan  B) Hospital indemnity policy  C) Disability income policy  D) Dread disease policy
> **B.** Hospital indemnity pays a fixed per-day amount independent of expenses incurred — a limited policy requiring MA's limited-coverage notice. (Outline 3.4)

**2.** During a sales presentation, a producer reassures a nervous client: "Even if this insurer fails, the state Guaranty Association will pay your claims." The producer has:
A) Properly disclosed a consumer protection  B) Violated 175:146B(19), which prohibits using the Association as a sales inducement  C) Committed twisting  D) Done nothing wrong if the statement is true
> **B.** The Guaranty Association exists, but producers are barred from advertising or using it to induce a sale — even truthfully. (175:146B(19))

**3.** Under Massachusetts law, an insurer underwriting an individual health application may:
A) Require a genetic test as a condition of issue  B) Classify risk based on sexual orientation  C) Order an HIV test with the applicant's written informed consent  D) Decline solely on an MIB code
> **C.** HIV testing is permitted only with written informed consent (211 CMR 36.05); genetic testing requirements are barred (175:108H–I); sexual orientation cannot be used; and MIB data alone cannot support a declination. (Module 3.7)

**4.** The producer takes an application but collects no premium. For coverage to begin at policy delivery, the producer must collect the premium AND obtain:
A) A new application  B) A statement of continued good health  C) An attending physician statement  D) A conditional receipt
> **B.** Without premium at application there is no interim coverage; at delivery the insurer requires payment plus a statement of continued good health. (Outline 3.6)

**5.** Marcus, 34, carries only a cancer-only dread disease policy. For Massachusetts individual mandate purposes, he:
A) Satisfies the mandate — any policy counts  B) Does not have Minimum Creditable Coverage and may owe a state tax penalty on Schedule HC  C) Is exempt because he is under 40  D) Satisfies the mandate if the policy is guaranteed renewable
> **B.** The MA mandate (c. 111M) requires comprehensive Minimum Creditable Coverage; limited accident/sickness policies don't qualify, and the penalty is assessed through the state income tax return. (956 CMR 5.00)

**6.** The Medical Information Bureau (MIB) is best described as:
A) A government registry of health claims  B) A nonprofit bureau of coded underwriting data shared by member insurers to deter misrepresentation  C) A credit reporting agency  D) The state's external review agency
> **B.** MIB is a member-insurer information exchange; applicants must authorize access, and adverse action can't rest on MIB codes alone. (Outline 3.7)

**7.** An applicant's statements on a health insurance application are legally considered:
A) Warranties — guaranteed true  B) Representations — true to the best of the applicant's knowledge  C) Concealments  D) Binding estimates
> **B.** Application statements are representations; only material misrepresentations affect recovery (ties to the § 108(5) rule from Module 4). (Outline 3.7)
