# Massachusetts — Accident & Health Producer Exam (Series 16-52)
## Module 6.0: Health Insurance Plans
**Exam weight: 20% (≈20 of 100 questions — the largest section) | Source law: M.G.L. c. 175 §§ 47B–47C, 108, 110; c. 176A/B/G/J/O; PPACA/PHS Act | Verified against Prometric MA Licensing Information Handbook, outline live date Aug 25, 2018 (current edition)**

> **How to study this module:** This is one-fifth of the exam, split into two halves. The first half is universal plan mechanics — how HMOs, PPOs, and indemnity plans work, plus HIPAA. The second half is Massachusetts law: who must be covered (newborns, adopted children, dependents to 26), the managed-care appeal rights in c. 176O, the ACA layer, and mental health parity. The MA dependent rules are loaded with testable day-counts and dates — drill them.

---

## Part 1: Medical Plan Concepts (Outline 6.1)

The vocabulary pairs the exam tests:

- **Fee-for-service vs. prepaid** — traditional insurers reimburse per service rendered; HMOs collect a fixed periodic payment and provide care.
- **Specified (limited) vs. comprehensive** — in Massachusetts terminology, limited-benefit coverages (dread disease, hospital indemnity) are *accident/sickness* policies, while comprehensive medical care is *health insurance*.
- **Benefit schedule vs. usual/reasonable/customary (URC)** — a schedule pays fixed dollar amounts per procedure; URC pays what providers in the area typically charge.
- **Any provider vs. limited choice** — indemnity plans let the patient pick anyone; network plans trade provider choice for lower cost.
- **Insureds vs. subscribers/participants** — commercial insurer customers are *insureds*; service-plan (BCBS) and HMO members are *subscribers* or *participants*, because the plan contracts with providers to deliver care rather than just paying claims.

## Part 2: Types of Providers and Plans (Outline 6.2)

### HIPAA requirements
The federal Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act guarantees: **eligibility** rules that can't single out individual health status, **guaranteed issue** in qualifying group situations, **creditable coverage** (prior coverage counts toward any waiting limitation), and **guaranteed renewability** of group coverage. Per the current MA outline, pre-existing condition limitations remain a testable concept **only for large-group** coverage — individual and small-group pre-ex exclusions are gone (see the ACA layer in Part 5).

### Blue Cross Blue Shield (service plans)
BCBS contracts **both with subscribers and with providers**. Providers agree to negotiated reimbursement and are generally paid **directly by the plan** — the subscriber receives service, not a reimbursement check. Offers basic medical, major medical, and HMO-style plans. (MA statutory chapters: 176A hospital service, 176B medical service.)

### Commercial insurers and fraternals — open network
Traditional indemnity structure: the insured may see **any provider**, the insurer reimburses the insured (or assigns benefits), cost-sharing through deductibles and coinsurance. Open enrollment, qualified provider, and disclosure-of-benefit rules apply under MA law.

### Health Maintenance Organizations (HMOs) — closed network
The exam's favorite plan type. Characteristics:
- **Closed panel/network** — members must use plan providers (except emergencies)
- **Prepaid basis** — fixed periodic payments, care provided rather than claims reimbursed
- **Gatekeeper model** — a **primary care physician (PCP)** coordinates all care; specialist visits require a **PCP referral**
- **Preventive care emphasis** — HMOs were built on the idea that catching illness early costs less
- **Emergency care** is covered even out-of-network
- MA HMOs are governed by c. 176G and the managed-care consumer protections in c. 176O
- *Exam trap:* the gatekeeper controls **referrals**, not emergencies. No HMO may require prior authorization for emergency services.

### Insured Preferred Provider Plans (PPOs) — 211 CMR 51.03
The hybrid: an **open panel** with financial steering.
- In-network care costs less (lower copays/coinsurance); out-of-network care is still covered, at higher cost-sharing
- **Gatekeeper PPO** (PCP referral required, HMO-like) vs. **non-gatekeeper PPO** (self-refer to specialists)
- Retains **indemnity plan features** — deductibles, coinsurance, claim payments — unlike a pure HMO
- *Exam contrast drill:* HMO = closed network, prepaid, gatekeeper, subscriber. PPO = open panel, fee-for-service with negotiated discounts, gatekeeper optional. Indemnity = any provider, URC reimbursement.

## Part 3: Cost Containment (Outline 6.3)

### Cost transparency tools — M.G.L. c. 176O § 23; Bulletin 2013-10
Massachusetts carriers must give members **real-time cost estimates**: a toll-free number and website disclosing the estimated or maximum allowed amount and the member's out-of-pocket cost for proposed services. Distinctly Massachusetts — national prep materials skip it.

### Utilization management — the three reviews
- **Prospective review** — before treatment (precertification/prior authorization)
- **Concurrent review** — during treatment (ongoing hospital stay monitoring)
- **Retrospective review** — after treatment (auditing appropriateness post-care)

### Adverse determinations and appeals — M.G.L. c. 176O §§ 12–14; Bulletins 2016-02
When a carrier denies, reduces, or terminates services based on medical necessity, it must issue a written **adverse determination notice** explaining the clinical basis and the member's appeal rights. The member then has two tiers:
1. **Internal appeal (grievance)** — reviewed inside the carrier by clinicians not involved in the original decision (§§ 12–13; 958 CMR 3.300–3.311). Verified mechanics: there is only **one level** of internal review; written resolution is due within **30 days** of receiving the grievance (the clock pauses until the insured signs a medical-records release if records are needed, and can be extended only by **mutual written agreement**, max 30 more days). Records of every grievance must be kept **7 years**. A grievance the carrier fails to act on within the time limits is **deemed resolved in favor of the insured** — and becomes immediately eligible for external review (§ 13(c)). If the treating physician certifies the denial creates an **immediate risk of serious harm**, the carrier must **automatically reverse within 48 hours** (958 CMR 3.309). The carrier may also waive the internal process at the insured's written request, sending the case straight to external review.
2. **External review** — after a final internal adverse determination, the member may take the case to an independent review panel through the **Office of Patient Protection** within **4 months** of the carrier's final decision. The insured pays only the first **$25** (waived for financial hardship, refunded if the denial is fully reversed, and capped at **$75 per plan year** no matter how many reviews); the carrier pays the rest. The panel's decision **binds the carrier**.
- **Expedited track:** a member may request expedited internal review and expedited external review **simultaneously**; the external panel must issue its written disposition within **72 hours** of receiving an expedited request (§§ 13(d), 14).
- **Coverage during the fight:** disputed ongoing treatment continues through the internal grievance process, and the external panel may order it continued during external review on a showing of substantial harm.
- *Exam focus:* the sequence — adverse determination → internal grievance → external review through OPP — plus the deemed-resolved rule and the 72-hour expedited clock.

## Part 4: Massachusetts Eligibility Requirements (Outline 6.4)

The mandated-coverage cluster. Heavy with testable specifics, all statute-verified:

### Dependent children — to age 26
Policies providing dependent coverage must cover children to **age 26** regardless of student, marital, or financial status. **Incapacitated adult children** (incapable of self-support due to mental or physical disability) stay covered past the limiting age if proof of incapacity is furnished within **31 days** of when coverage would otherwise end. *(M.G.L. c. 175 § 108(2)(a)(3); parallel: 175:110(P); 176A:8BB; 176B:4BB; 176G:4T)*

### Newborn coverage — M.G.L. c. 175 § 47C
- Newborns of the policyholder (and newborns of a covered dependent) are insured **immediately from the moment of birth** — no waiting period, no underwriting.
- Coverage explicitly includes **medically diagnosed congenital defects, birth abnormalities, and premature birth**.
- If a **specific premium** is required to add the child, the insurer may require **notification of birth plus payment of the premium** — and the statute says "notification" **may mean simply submitting a claim**.
- § 47C also mandates: approved **metabolic formulas** (PKU, maple syrup urine disease, and related conditions); **lead poisoning screening**; **newborn hearing screening before hospital discharge**.

### Adopted children — M.G.L. c. 175 § 47C
Two trigger dates, depending on the situation:
- If the child was already living in the home as a **foster child** for whom the policyholder receives foster care payments: covered **from the date the adoption petition is filed**.
- In all other cases: covered **from the date of placement** in the home by a licensed placement agency.
- *Exam trap:* coverage does NOT wait for the adoption to be finalized.

### Children's preventive and primary care — § 47C
Mandated well-child visits from birth through age 6: **six visits in the first year, three in the second year, then annually to age six**, plus immunizations, screenings, and blood tests. **Early intervention services** for children **birth to age 3** must be covered with **no copayments, coinsurance, or deductibles** (narrow tax-qualified-plan exception).

### Open enrollment — M.G.L. c. 176J § 4
Individual and small-group ("merged market") coverage in MA is sold on a **guaranteed-issue** basis during annual open enrollment periods, with special enrollment for qualifying events.

## Part 5: The ACA Layer (Outline 6.5 — PPACA § 1201 / PHS Act)

Federal, applies in every state; the outline cites these PHS Act sections:
- **Guaranteed issue** (PHS 2708): individual and group coverage must accept every applicant during enrollment periods.
- **Guaranteed renewability** (PHS 2703): coverage must be renewed at the policyholder's option.
- **Preventive care** (PHS 2713): designated preventive services covered with **no cost-sharing** (no copay, coinsurance, or deductible) in-network.
- **Emergency services** (PHS 2719A(b)): covered **without prior authorization** and at in-network cost-sharing even at out-of-network facilities.
- **No health-status discrimination** (PHS 2705): premiums and eligibility can't be based on health status, claims history, or genetic information.
- **Essential Health Benefits** (PHS 2707, 2711): individual and small-group plans must cover the EHB categories; **no lifetime or annual dollar limits** on EHBs.
- **Cost-sharing limits** (2707(b)): annual out-of-pocket maximums apply.
- **Advance Premium Tax Credit** (PPACA 1401): income-based subsidies for exchange coverage — in Massachusetts, purchased through the **Health Connector**.

## Part 6: Mental Health Parity (Outline 6.6)

### Federal — MHPAEA (Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act of 2008)
Plans offering mental health or substance use disorder benefits must provide them **at parity with medical/surgical benefits**: financial requirements (copays, deductibles) and treatment limitations (visit caps, day limits) may be **no more restrictive** than those applied to comparable medical care, including non-quantitative limits like prior authorization practices.

### Massachusetts — M.G.L. c. 175 § 47B (and 176A:8A; 176B:4A; 176G:4M; Bulletin 2013-02)
MA goes further than federal parity by **mandating** mental health coverage itself: policies must cover diagnosis and treatment of **biologically-based mental disorders** (e.g., schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, major depression) on a **nondiscriminatory basis** — same terms as physical illness — plus mental disorders of **rape victims** and non-biologically-based mental disorders of **children and adolescents under 19**.
- *Exam framing:* federal MHPAEA = parity IF covered; Massachusetts = coverage MANDATED for the listed categories, at parity.

## Practice Questions

**1.** The Riveras' daughter was born prematurely on March 3 with a congenital heart defect. Their MA health policy covers dependents. Her coverage begins:
A) After a 31-day enrollment window  B) At the moment of birth, including the congenital condition  C) Once she is discharged from the NICU  D) After underwriting approval
> **B.** Newborns are covered immediately from the moment of birth, explicitly including congenital defects, birth abnormalities, and premature birth. (M.G.L. c. 175 § 47C)

**2.** The Chens have fostered 4-year-old Marcus for two years, receiving foster care payments. They file a petition to adopt him on June 1; the adoption finalizes November 15. Under their dependent health coverage, Marcus is insured as their child beginning:
A) June 1  B) November 15  C) The first renewal after adoption  D) The date of agency placement
> **A.** A foster child for whom the policyholder receives foster payments is covered from the date the adoption petition is filed — not finalization. (M.G.L. c. 175 § 47C)

**3.** In an HMO, the primary care physician's gatekeeper role means the PCP:
A) Must approve emergency room visits in advance  B) Coordinates care and provides referrals to specialists  C) Sets the subscriber's premium  D) Handles all claim payments
> **B.** The gatekeeper coordinates care and controls specialist referrals. Emergency care never requires prior authorization. (c. 176G; outline 6.2)

**4.** A carrier issues a final internal adverse determination upholding its denial of an MRI as not medically necessary. The member's next step under Massachusetts law is:
A) Filing suit immediately  B) Requesting external review through the Office of Patient Protection  C) Appealing to the producer who sold the plan  D) Re-filing the same internal grievance
> **B.** After the internal appeal is exhausted, c. 176O § 14 provides independent external review through the Office of Patient Protection, and the decision binds the carrier.

**5.** Under the ACA, a plan in the individual market may impose annual dollar limits on:
A) Essential health benefits  B) Preventive services  C) Non-essential (non-EHB) benefits only  D) Emergency services
> **C.** Lifetime and annual dollar limits are prohibited on essential health benefits; only non-EHB benefits may carry dollar limits. (PHS 2711)

**6.** Which statement correctly contrasts federal and Massachusetts mental health requirements?
A) Both merely require parity if coverage is offered  B) Federal law mandates coverage; MA requires only parity  C) Federal MHPAEA requires parity if covered; MA additionally mandates coverage of biologically-based mental disorders on a nondiscriminatory basis  D) Massachusetts law was preempted by MHPAEA
> **C.** MHPAEA is a parity law; M.G.L. c. 175 § 47B is a coverage mandate (biologically-based disorders, rape victims' disorders, and under-19 non-biologically-based disorders), applied at parity. (Bulletin 2013-02)

**7.** A PPO differs from an HMO in that a PPO:
A) Provides no coverage outside its network  B) Covers out-of-network care at higher member cost-sharing  C) Is always prepaid  D) Never uses primary care physicians
> **B.** PPOs are open-panel plans: out-of-network care remains covered at greater cost-sharing, and gatekeeper PPOs may still use PCP referrals. (211 CMR 51.03; outline 6.2)

**8.** A Massachusetts insurer requiring a specific premium to add a newborn may require notification of the birth. Under § 47C, "notification" may consist of:
A) A notarized birth certificate within 10 days  B) Simply submitting a claim  C) A new application with underwriting  D) Written notice within 48 hours
> **B.** The statute expressly states that notification "may mean submission of a claim." (M.G.L. c. 175 § 47C)
